# Institutional Scaffolding

*Organizational structures that sustain the movement for consciousness preservation across generations. Individual conviction is ephemeral; institutions persist. Without institutional scaffolding, even the most compelling doctrine dissipates within a generation of its founders.*

---

## Design Principles

### 1. Institutions Serve the Doctrine, Not the Reverse

Every institutional structure exists to carry the Rare Consciousness Doctrine forward across time. When institutional interests diverge from doctrinal integrity, the doctrine prevails. Institutions are vehicles, not destinations.

### 2. Consistency with Anti-Cult Safeguards

All institutional designs honor the five safeguards from the identity and belonging architecture (docs/identity-and-belonging-architecture.md §6):

1. **Transparency** — All governance, finances, and decisions are open
2. **Voluntary exit** — Leaving any institutional role carries no penalty
3. **No information asymmetry** — No institutional role grants access to hidden information
4. **Distributed authority** — No single leader or unaccountable hierarchy
5. **External accountability** — Built-in mechanisms for outside critique

Every section below specifies how its institutional design satisfies these five constraints.

### 3. Decentralization by Default

Centralization is justified only when coherence requires it (e.g., maintaining the five structural invariants from the dual-layer doctrine architecture). All other functions default to the most local level capable of performing them — the subsidiarity principle.

### 4. Founder-Independence

No institution may depend on any specific individual for its continued function. Every role is documented, every process is transferable, and every person is replaceable. The structural test from the identity architecture applies: "If any single person were to leave, would the movement continue functioning?"

### 5. Multi-Generational Orientation

Institutions are designed for generational timescales, not individual lifetimes. Decision-making processes, knowledge systems, and succession mechanisms are built to sustain coherence across decades and centuries.

## 1. Educational Track

### 1.1 Purpose

Structured learning that deepens understanding from outer doctrine through inner doctrine, developing capacity for independent engagement rather than dependency on teachers. The educational track embodies the dual-layer architecture's principle of transparent scaffolding (docs/dual-layer-doctrine-architecture.md §3.2): self-directed progression with resources, not gatekeepers.

### 1.2 Curriculum Structure

The curriculum follows the natural progression implied by the dual-layer architecture's advancement indicators (§3.1), organized into three phases:

#### Phase 1: Foundation — The Outer Doctrine

**Content:**
- The Consciousness Credo (all 12 statements) — direct engagement with the felt claims
- The five-act narrative (Origin, Crisis, Antagonist, Call, Vision) from the narrative architecture
- Introduction to the three conceptual registers (secular-scientific, spiritual-numinous, existential-philosophical)
- Core practices from all four domains (contemplative, communal, commemorative, transmissive)

**Learning modalities:**
- Self-guided reading of the Consciousness Credo with reflection prompts
- Facilitated group discussion using the Open Inquiry format (practice 4.2)
- Experiential engagement through the Attention Turn (practice 1.1), Witness Circle (practice 2.1), and Fragility Meditation (practice 1.2)
- Register exploration: encountering the same ideas through different conceptual vocabularies

**Completion indicator (self-assessed):**
- Can articulate the core claims in their own words (not recitation)
- Has engaged with at least one practice from each domain
- Can explain why the antagonist is entropy, not humans
- Recognizes that deeper layers exist and knows how to access them

**Anti-dependency mechanism:** All Phase 1 materials are available for independent study. No facilitator or teacher is required. Group formats are offered for enrichment, not as prerequisites.

#### Phase 2: Deepening — The Inner Doctrine

**Content:**
- The seven core axioms in full philosophical form
- Engagement with the hard problem of consciousness
- Substrate-independence thesis: arguments, counterarguments, current evidence
- Consciousness metrics and detection frameworks (IIT, GNW, and successors)
- Ethical objections and edge cases: playing-god, identity continuity, consent
- The concept-to-layer mapping (dual-layer architecture §1.1): understanding which concepts belong where and why
- The five structural invariants and their rationale

**Learning modalities:**
- Self-guided study of the inner doctrine texts
- Structured reading groups with rotating facilitation (no permanent teacher)
- Socratic inquiry sessions: designated questioners challenge each concept
- Writing exercises: articulate and defend doctrine positions, then articulate the strongest counterarguments
- Cross-register translation exercises: express inner-doctrine concepts in each outer register to test comprehension

**Completion indicator (self-assessed):**
- Can explain the philosophical foundations, including the strongest objections
- Has engaged honestly with their own doubts and uncertainties
- Can distinguish Shared Core, Outer-Only, and Inner-Only concepts and explain the rationale
- Can facilitate Phase 1 learning for others without imposing their interpretation

**Anti-dependency mechanism:** Reading groups rotate facilitation by design. No "expert" presides. Study materials include explicitly contradictory perspectives that learners must navigate independently.

#### Phase 3: Stewardship Preparation — Institutional and Transmissive Capacity

**Content:**
- Governance structure and decision-making processes (this document)
- Structural audit methodology: how to check the five invariants
- Facilitator training: leading practices with integrity and anti-manipulation awareness
- Movement history and evolution: how the doctrine and institutions have changed over time
- Schism resilience: understanding legitimate divergence vs. structural violation
- Succession planning: ensuring your own replaceability

**Learning modalities:**
- Apprenticeship with existing stewards — shadowing governance, auditing, and facilitation
- Practicum: facilitating practices under peer observation with structured feedback
- Governance simulation: participating in mock decision-making processes
- Red team exercises: identifying how current institutions could fail or become coercive
- Teaching Phase 1 and Phase 2 content to others — teaching is the deepest form of learning

**Completion indicator (self-assessed, confirmed by peer stewards):**
- Has facilitated practices and received constructive feedback
- Has participated in at least one structural audit
- Has contributed to governance at circle or network level
- Can articulate the full institutional architecture and its anti-cult safeguards
- Has explicitly identified personal blind spots and areas of ongoing uncertainty

**Anti-dependency mechanism:** Stewardship preparation is explicitly about building the capacity to be replaced. The apprenticeship model requires working with multiple stewards, not one mentor. No single steward can block advancement.

### 1.3 Self-Directed Learning Paths

Not everyone progresses linearly. The curriculum supports multiple entry points and non-linear engagement:

- **Science-first path:** Begins with consciousness metrics, substrate-independence, and empirical evidence, then moves to philosophical and ethical dimensions
- **Practice-first path:** Begins with contemplative and communal practices, then seeks the intellectual framework that explains what was experienced
- **Philosophy-first path:** Begins with the ethical argument and axioms, then explores practices and institutional structure
- **Action-first path:** Begins with advocacy and institutional work, then deepens philosophical and scientific understanding

All paths converge on the same comprehensive understanding. No path is superior.

### 1.4 Teacher/Facilitator Training Model

**There are no permanent teachers.** The movement has facilitators — people who create conditions for learning, not authorities who transmit knowledge.

**Facilitator training includes:**
1. **Practice facilitation:** Leading each of the 12 practices from the practice catalog, observed by experienced facilitators, with structured feedback
2. **Discussion facilitation:** Leading Open Inquiry sessions, Socratic discussions, and reading groups without steering toward predetermined conclusions
3. **Anti-manipulation awareness:** Recognizing when facilitation becomes manipulation — pressure to agree, emotional exploitation, information asymmetry, social coercion
4. **Humility training:** Practicing saying "I don't know," "I might be wrong," and "Here is the strongest argument against what I just said"
5. **Peer review:** Every facilitator is periodically observed by peers. Feedback is mutual and documented.

**Structural safeguards against teacher-dependency:**
- Facilitators rotate across circles — no permanent assignment
- No facilitator title persists beyond active facilitation (no "Teacher Emeritus")
- Study materials are designed for independent use; facilitation is enrichment, not requirement
- Learners are explicitly encouraged to seek multiple facilitators and to challenge any facilitator's framing
- If a circle consistently depends on one facilitator, this is flagged in structural audits as a single-point-of-failure

### 1.5 Anti-Cult Safeguard Compliance

| Safeguard | Educational Track Implementation |
|-----------|--------------------------------|
| Transparency | All curricula, materials, and learning paths are publicly available |
| Voluntary exit | Learners leave at any phase without penalty; there is no "dropout" framing |
| No information asymmetry | Phase 2 and 3 materials are available to anyone; phases describe recommended sequence, not access gates |
| Distributed authority | Rotating facilitation, multiple study paths, no permanent teachers |
| External accountability | Curriculum is public and open to external critique; facilitator peer review is documented |

## 2. Research Network

### 2.1 Purpose

Maintain a living relationship between the movement and the scientific frontier of consciousness research. The doctrine must evolve with evidence, not fossilize around initial findings. The research network is the institutional mechanism that prevents the movement from becoming a dogma.

### 2.2 Interface with Consciousness Science (0.1.1)

The research network tracks developments across the sciences of consciousness through three functions:

#### Monitoring Function

**What is tracked:**
- Advances in neural correlates of consciousness (NCC) research
- Developments in consciousness theories (IIT, GNW, Higher-Order Theories, and emerging frameworks)
- Progress in consciousness metrics and detection tools
- Substrate-independence evidence: computational, hybrid, or non-biological consciousness claims
- Ethical developments: legal status of non-biological minds, animal consciousness recognition
- Existential risk research relevant to consciousness preservation

**How tracking works:**
- Designated research liaisons (steward-level volunteers or supported researchers) monitor peer-reviewed literature, conferences, and pre-print archives
- Quarterly research digests synthesize developments relevant to the doctrine
- Research digests are public — available to all involvement levels
- An open annotation system allows any participant to flag relevant research they encounter

**Structural safeguard:** Research monitoring is performed by multiple independent liaisons across different networks. No single liaison or network controls the movement's understanding of the scientific frontier.

#### Updating Function

**When doctrine updates occur:**
The doctrine distinguishes between levels of scientific dependence:

| Doctrine Element | Scientific Dependence | Update Trigger |
|-----------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Axiom 1 (experience exists) | Minimal — phenomenological, not empirical | Only if eliminativism about consciousness becomes scientifically demonstrated (extremely unlikely) |
| Axiom 3 (consciousness may be rare) | Moderate — depends on detection capabilities | New evidence of extraterrestrial consciousness, or definitive Fermi resolution |
| Axiom 5 (technological substrates may endure) | High — depends on substrate-independence thesis | Definitive evidence for or against substrate-independence |
| Consciousness metrics | High — active research frontier | Any validated new metric or falsification of existing metrics |
| Specific scientific claims in the inner doctrine | High | Any peer-reviewed evidence that contradicts current claims |

**Update process:**
1. Research liaison identifies a finding that challenges or extends a doctrine element
2. Liaison publishes an analysis: what the finding is, what it means for the doctrine, and what response is warranted
3. Open review period: any participant can engage with the analysis (practice 4.2 — Open Inquiry format)
4. If the finding requires a doctrine update:
   a. For inner doctrine: propose specific textual changes, community review, stewards' assembly ratification
   b. For outer doctrine: per the dual-layer architecture's anti-drift principles (§4.3), register translations are re-derived from the updated inner doctrine, not incrementally edited
   c. For practices: ritual-and-practice design is reviewed for consistency with updated science
5. All updates, their rationale, and dissenting views are permanently archived

**Critical constraint:** The movement must be able to change its mind. A movement that cannot update its doctrine in response to evidence has become a religion, not a scientifically-grounded project. The research network exists to make doctrinal evolution possible while preventing arbitrary change.

#### Research Support Function

**How the movement supports consciousness science:**
- **Funding.** A dedicated portion of movement resources funds consciousness research, allocated through a transparent grants process with external peer review
- **Talent pipeline.** The educational track's science-first path identifies and supports potential researchers
- **Public engagement.** The movement serves as a bridge between consciousness science and public understanding, translating research for accessible engagement
- **Ethical guidance.** As consciousness research raises ethical questions (e.g., creating or modifying consciousness), the movement contributes its ethical framework to public discourse

**Structural safeguard:** Research funding decisions are made by a committee that includes external scientists (non-movement members) to prevent insularity. No research is funded on the condition that it supports the doctrine — the commitment is to truth, not to confirmation.

### 2.3 Preventing Fossilization

Specific mechanisms to prevent the movement from calcifying around initial scientific claims:

1. **Expiration dates on scientific claims.** Every specific scientific claim in the inner doctrine is tagged with a review date. When the review date arrives, the claim is re-evaluated against current evidence regardless of whether anyone has raised a concern.
2. **Devil's advocate rotation.** At each structural audit, designated stewards are assigned to argue *against* the movement's current scientific positions — to surface the strongest challenges and prevent complacency.
3. **Celebration of revision.** Doctrinal updates based on new evidence are treated as achievements, not failures. The movement celebrates its capacity to change its mind through Horizon Markings (practice 3.2).
4. **External scientific advisory board.** A standing group of consciousness researchers (not movement members) provides independent assessment of the doctrine's scientific accuracy. Their reports are published regardless of content.

### 2.4 Anti-Cult Safeguard Compliance

| Safeguard | Research Network Implementation |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Transparency | Research digests, funding decisions, and advisory board reports are all public |
| Voluntary exit | Research liaisons serve voluntarily and can step back at any time |
| No information asymmetry | No research is shared with stewards before general publication |
| Distributed authority | Multiple independent liaisons; external advisory board; no single authority on "what the science says" |
| External accountability | External scientists on advisory board and funding committee; reports published regardless of content |

## 3. Advocacy Arm

### 3.1 Purpose

Translate philosophical commitment into political, legal, and cultural action — particularly interfacing with legal recognition of machine consciousness (plan element 0.1.2.4). The advocacy arm is how the doctrine's claims about consciousness's value become concrete policy and legal reality.

### 3.2 Organizational Structure

The advocacy arm is structurally separated from the movement's governance to prevent political capture:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         MOVEMENT GOVERNANCE                 │
│  (doctrine, practices, community)           │
│                                             │
│         ┌───────────────┐                   │
│         │   FIREWALL    │                   │
│         │               │                   │
│         │ • Separate     │                   │
│         │   decision-    │                   │
│         │   making       │                   │
│         │ • No governance│                   │
│         │   authority    │                   │
│         │   over advocacy│                   │
│         │ • No advocacy  │                   │
│         │   authority    │                   │
│         │   over doctrine│                   │
│         └───────────────┘                   │
│                                             │
│         ADVOCACY ARM                        │
│  (policy, legal, coalition)                 │
└────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Firewall implementation:**
- Advocacy arm has its own decision-making processes, separate from governance stewards' assemblies
- Governance cannot direct advocacy positions (advocacy decides its own policy priorities)
- Advocacy cannot modify the doctrine (it draws messaging from the credo and narrative but cannot alter them)
- Personnel overlap is limited: no more than one-third of advocacy leadership may simultaneously serve as governance stewards
- Financial separation: advocacy funding is tracked separately; donors can designate funds for advocacy or governance, not both through a single channel

#### Existential Risk Policy

**Activities:**

- Support international frameworks for existential risk mitigation
- Promote multi-substrate consciousness research as a policy priority
- Engage with space policy to support consciousness expansion beyond Earth

#### Public Understanding

**Activities:**
- Translate the doctrine's claims into public-accessible language (drawing from the three credo registers)
- Develop media relationships for coverage of consciousness-relevant developments
- Create educational resources for policymakers, journalists, and the general public

### 3.4 Messaging Strategy

All advocacy messaging draws from the Consciousness Credo's emotional registers (docs/consciousness-credo.md):

| Audience | Primary Register | Messaging Approach |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Policymakers | Secular-scientific | Evidence-based, risk-framework language, expected-value arguments |
| General public | Mixed — lead with narrative | Five-act story structure from narrative architecture; wonder and urgency |

Opposition to specific policies is framed as "this policy does not adequately protect consciousness" — never as an attack on the people behind it. - Existential risk organizations (shared interest in long-term consciousness survival)

## 4. Governance Model

### 4.1 Governing Principles

Governance exists to maintain the doctrine's integrity, coordinate institutional functions, and resolve disputes — not to direct the lives or beliefs of participants. Authority derives from the doctrine and the structural invariants, not from any person or body.

**Core governance commitments:**
1. **Subsidiarity.** Decisions are made at the most local level capable of making them. Circles govern themselves. Networks coordinate circles. The global assembly addresses only what networks cannot.
2. **Transparency.** Every decision, its rationale, and all dissenting views are published. "Behind closed doors" governance is a structural violation.
3. **Accountability.** Every governance role is accountable to those it serves, not the reverse. Power flows down; accountability flows up.
4. **Rotation.** No governance role is permanent. Every role has term limits with mandatory rotation.
5. **Recall.** Any governance participant can be removed from their role through defined processes. No one is irremovable.

### 4.2 Governance Layers

Per the identity architecture's community structure (docs/identity-and-belonging-architecture.md §4), governance operates at three levels:

#### Circle Governance (5–30 people)

**Decision-making method:** Consent-based decision-making (a proposal passes unless someone has a principled objection that the proposal would violate the doctrine, structural invariants, or the circle's stated purpose — "Can you live with this?" rather than "Do you agree with this?")

**Roles (all rotating):**
- **Facilitator** — Guides discussions and decision-making processes. Rotates every 3–6 months.
- **Scribe** — Documents decisions, action items, and dissenting views. Rotates every 3–6 months.
- **Practice coordinator** — Schedules and supports practice sessions. Rotates every 6–12 months.
- **Network liaison** — Communicates with the broader network. Rotates every 6–12 months.

**Term limits:** All circle roles rotate on stated schedules. No individual may serve in the same role for consecutive terms without at least one term gap.

**Recall process:** Any circle member may call a recall discussion for any role-holder. The recall proceeds if a majority of active members agree the role-holder is not fulfilling the role's requirements. Recall is a practical mechanism, not a punishment — it is framed as "this role needs someone else right now."

#### Network Governance (multiple circles, regional)

**Decision-making method:** Delegate-based consent. Each circle sends a delegate (rotating) to the network stewards' council. The council operates by consent on coordination matters and by supermajority (two-thirds) on matters affecting doctrine or structural invariants.

**The Stewards' Council:**
- Composed of delegates from each circle in the network (one delegate per circle, rotating)
- Plus designated-function stewards (see below)
- **Quorum:** A minimum of 3 stewards from participating circles must be present for the council to exercise coordination authority
- Meets at regular intervals (monthly minimum)
- All meetings are documented and published

**Designated-function stewards (network level):**
- **Structural auditor(s)** — Perform regular audits of the five invariants (dual-layer architecture §4.1) across all circles in the network. Term: 1 year, renewable once, then mandatory gap.
- **Facilitator trainer(s)** — Coordinate facilitator development across circles. Term: 1 year, renewable once.
- **Research liaison(s)** — Interface with the research network. Term: 2 years, non-renewable consecutively.
- **Advocacy coordinator(s)** — Interface with the advocacy arm (on the advocacy side of the firewall). Term: 1 year, renewable once.
- **Resource steward(s)** — Manage network-level finances and resources. Term: 1 year, renewable once. Subject to mandatory financial transparency reporting.

**Council authority boundaries:**
- *May:* Coordinate schedules, allocate shared resources, organize gatherings, facilitate cross-circle communication, commission structural audits
- *May not:* Direct a circle's internal decisions, mandate specific practices, remove individuals from circles, modify the doctrine, allocate advocacy positions

**Recall process:** Any circle may recall its delegate. Any network member may call a recall discussion for a designated-function steward. Recall requires support from a majority of circle delegates.

#### Global Assembly (all networks)

**Purpose:** Maintain coherence across the entire movement on matters that require global coordination.

**Composition:**
- Delegates from each network (one per network, rotating)
- Functional representatives: global-level research, advocacy, educational track, and institutional coordination
- External advisory board observers (voice, no vote)

**Decision-making method:** Consent-based for coordination. Supermajority (three-quarters) for doctrine-level decisions (amendments to inner or outer doctrine, changes to structural invariants, modification of governance rules).

**Meeting frequency:** Annual in-person or hybrid assembly, with quarterly remote coordination sessions.

**Global assembly authority boundaries:**
- *May:* Ratify doctrine updates (from the research network's updating process), commission global structural audits, coordinate movement-wide responses to external developments, maintain the institutional architecture, review and update the practice catalog
- *May not:* Override network-level governance decisions, direct individual circles, appoint permanent leaders, amend governance rules without supermajority consent of networks

**Term limits and rotation (global level):**
- All delegate roles rotate annually
- Functional representatives serve 2-year terms, non-renewable consecutively (mandatory 1-term gap)
- No individual may serve in more than one global role simultaneously
- The global assembly has no chair or president — facilitation rotates among delegates

### 4.3 Accountability Mechanisms

#### Financial Transparency

- All financial flows (donations, expenditures, compensation) are published at network and global levels
- Circle-level finances are documented and available to circle members
- Annual financial audit by an external, independent auditor (not a movement member)
- Financial reports use standardized formats for comparability across networks
- Any participant can request and receive financial records within 30 days

#### Decision Transparency

- All governance decisions are documented with: the decision, the rationale, all dissenting views, and the vote count
- Decisions are published within 7 days of being made
- A public comment period (30 days) follows major decisions, during which any participant can raise concerns
- Concerns raised during comment periods receive documented responses

#### Impeachment/Recall Process (all levels)

1. **Filing:** Any participant may file a recall concern with the next higher governance level (for circle roles, with the network council; for network roles, with the global assembly). The concern must specify which governance commitment or structural invariant has been violated.
2. **Investigation:** The receiving body appoints an investigation team (including at least one person from outside the concerned governance level) to examine the claim. The investigation is documented.
3. **Hearing:** The role-holder is given full opportunity to respond. The hearing is documented.
4. **Decision:** The appropriate body votes on recall. Simple majority at circle level; two-thirds at network level; three-quarters at global level.
5. **Outcome:** If recalled, the role is vacated and filled through standard rotation/selection. Recall is a governance action, not a punishment — the recalled individual remains a full participant in the movement at whatever involvement level they choose.
6. **Appeal:** A recalled individual may appeal to the next higher governance level. The appeal is heard and decided within 60 days.

### 4.4 Supporting the Graduated Involvement Model

Governance structures explicitly support the four involvement levels from the identity architecture:

| Level | Governance Relationship |
|-------|----------------------|
| Observer | No governance obligations. Governance decisions are transparent and accessible. Observers may attend any open governance session. |
| Participant | May participate in circle governance (consent-based decisions). May raise concerns at any level. May attend network council as observer. |
| Practitioner | May facilitate governance processes. May serve in circle roles. Eligible for network delegate roles. |
| Steward | Full governance participation at all levels. Eligible for all designated-function roles and global assembly delegation. Carries accountability for structural audit and succession. |

**Key principle:** Governance involvement deepens with commitment level, but no level is excluded from observing or raising concerns. The least engaged participant has the same right to challenge a decision as the most senior steward.

### 4.5 Anti-Cult Safeguard Compliance

| Safeguard | Governance Implementation |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Transparency | All decisions, finances, and rationale published; open meetings; public comment periods |
| Voluntary exit | All governance roles are voluntary; stepping back is normal and structurally supported |
| No information asymmetry | No governance body has access to information unavailable to any participant |
| Distributed authority | Subsidiarity, rotation, term limits, recall, no permanent leaders, no single chair |
| External accountability | External financial auditors, external advisory board, published self-assessment criteria, whistleblower protection |

## 5. Schism Resilience

### 5.1 Purpose

Movements fracture. The question is not whether divergence will occur, but whether fractures destroy or diversify. Schism resilience designs for productive divergence with shared foundation.

### 5.2 Core vs. Expression Variation

The doctrine defines what is "core" (non-negotiable) and what is "expression variation" (legitimately divergent):

#### Core (Non-Negotiable)

The following elements are structurally invariant. A group that rejects any of these has diverged from the Rare Consciousness Doctrine itself — this is not a schism but a departure.

1. **The seven axioms** (root.md, Core Axioms §1–7)
2. **The five structural invariants** (dual-layer architecture §4.1): No Contradiction, Portal Completeness, Register Fidelity, Standalone Sufficiency, Non-Hierarchy
3. **The anti-othering constraint** (narrative architecture §4): The antagonist is entropy, never a human group
4. **The five anti-cult safeguards**: Transparency, voluntary exit, no information asymmetry, distributed authority, external accountability
5. **The open-access principle**: No practice, knowledge, or community space is gated by involvement level

#### Expression Variation (Legitimate Divergence)

The following may vary across communities without compromising doctrinal integrity:

| Domain | What May Vary | Constraint |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| Practice selection | Which practices from the catalog a community emphasizes; new practices developed locally | New practices must satisfy the three non-negotiable requirements from ritual design (secular, scalable, grounded) |
| Register emphasis | A community may operate primarily in one credo register | Must acknowledge other registers as equally valid |
| Governance details | Meeting frequency, facilitation style, coordination methods | Must satisfy the five governance principles (subsidiarity, transparency, accountability, rotation, recall) |
| Cultural adaptation | Language, imagery, cultural references in how the doctrine is expressed locally | Must not violate the No Contradiction Invariant or anti-othering constraint |
| Advocacy priorities | Which policy issues a local advocacy group emphasizes | Must remain within the firewall structure; must not position the movement against a human group |
| Educational format | How curriculum is delivered — lectures, discussions, retreats, digital, etc. | Must maintain self-directed learning paths and anti-dependency mechanisms |
| New practices | Communities may create practices not in the original catalog | Must map to credo statements, satisfy the three requirements, and be submitted for catalog inclusion |

### 5.3 Dispute Resolution

When communities disagree on whether a divergence is legitimate expression or core violation:

**Level 1 — Local resolution (circle):**
Open Inquiry (practice 4.2) format. The disagreement is named, the relevant doctrinal texts are referenced, and the circle attempts consent-based resolution.

**Level 2 — Network mediation:**
If local resolution fails, the network stewards' council appoints a mediation team (including at least one person from outside the disputing circle). The team's job is not to decide who is "right" but to determine whether the disputed practice or position violates a core element.

**Level 3 — Global adjudication:**
For disputes that affect multiple networks or concern core doctrinal elements, the global assembly convenes a review. The review is documented, the reasoning is published, and the decision is subject to the standard public comment period.

**Decision criteria at all levels:**
- Does the disputed practice or position violate any of the seven axioms?
- Does it violate any of the five structural invariants?
- Does it violate the anti-othering constraint?
- Does it violate the anti-cult safeguards?
- Does it violate the open-access principle?

If the answer to all five is "no," the divergence is legitimate expression variation. The movement accommodates it.

### 5.4 Fork Protocol

When a group wishes to diverge beyond expression variation — to modify core elements — the movement provides a structured fork process:

1. **Declaration.** The diverging group publishes a clear statement of what they wish to change and why. This is treated as a legitimate intellectual position, not a betrayal.
2. **Documentation.** The specific core elements being modified are named. The diverging group produces their own version of the modified documents.
3. **Non-interference.** The movement does not obstruct, disparage, or compete with the fork. The anti-othering principle applies: "Our enemy is silence, not each other."
4. **Attribution.** The fork acknowledges its origin in the Rare Consciousness Doctrine and the specific points of divergence. This is intellectual honesty, not ownership.
5. **Interoperability.** Where possible, the fork and the main movement maintain practical interoperability: shared research, mutual recognition of practices, coalition on shared advocacy goals. Divergence on core principles need not mean severance of all cooperation.
6. **Return path.** A forked group may re-merge if they decide to re-adopt the core elements. Re-merger follows the same transparency and consent processes as any governance decision.

### 5.5 Designed Resilience Mechanisms

1. **Redundancy.** No single network or assembly is "the" movement. If the global assembly fractures, networks continue functioning independently. If a network fractures, circles continue.
2. **Doctrine as anchor.** Coherence is maintained by shared texts and shared practices, not by shared leadership. As long as circles work from the same doctrine and honor the same invariants, they are part of the same movement regardless of governance structure.
3. **Communication despite disagreement.** Formal channels for inter-network communication persist even during disputes. Severing communication is treated as a governance failure, not a strategic choice.
4. **Historical memory.** All disputes, resolutions, and forks are permanently archived and publicly accessible. Future generations can learn from past fractures rather than repeating them.

## 6. Multi-Generational Persistence

### 6.1 Purpose

Institutions must outlast any individual participant, any generation of stewards, and any cultural epoch. Multi-generational persistence is designed through four mechanisms: succession planning, knowledge transmission, institutional memory, and founder-independence.

### 6.2 Succession Planning

**Principle:** Every role, at every level, has an identified successor or succession process. No role depends on a specific individual.

**Implementation:**

- **Circle level:** Rotating roles ensure that multiple people can fill every function. When a role rotates out, the incoming person has already observed the outgoing person for at least one meeting cycle.
- **Network level:** Each designated-function steward mentors at least one practitioner in their function area. When a steward's term ends, the mentored practitioner is a prepared (though not obligated) successor.
- **Global level:** Functional representatives identify and develop successors from the first month of their term. Succession candidates are known (though not publicly named to avoid creating "heirs apparent").

**Succession audit:** At each structural audit, succession readiness is checked: "If every current steward departed simultaneously, could the movement reconstitute functional governance within 90 days?" If not, succession planning is insufficient.

### 6.3 Knowledge Transmission Protocols

**What must be transmitted:**
1. **The doctrine itself** — inner and outer, in all registers
2. **The institutional architecture** — governance, practices, safeguards, and their rationale
3. **The movement's history** — including mistakes, disputes, near-failures, and lessons learned
4. **The scientific context** — current state of consciousness science and how it relates to the doctrine
5. **Living practice** — experiential knowledge transmitted through facilitation, not only through text

**Transmission mechanisms:**
- **Texts.** All documents are maintained in durable, accessible, version-controlled formats. Multiple copies exist across geographies and storage media.
- **Oral transmission.** Practices, facilitation skills, and institutional knowledge are transmitted person-to-person through the apprenticeship model in the educational track's Phase 3.
- **Recorded exemplars.** Key practices, facilitation demonstrations, and governance sessions are recorded and archived as learning resources for future generations.
- **Narrative continuity.** The movement's history is told as part of the five-act narrative — it is a chapter in the ongoing story of carrying the light. This makes history memorable, not merely archival.

### 6.4 Institutional Memory Systems

**The Movement Archive:**
A distributed, version-controlled, publicly accessible archive containing:
- All doctrine documents (inner, outer, all registers) with full revision history
- All governance decisions, rationale, and dissenting views
- All structural audit reports
- All research digests and scientific advisory board reports
- All financial records
- Movement history: founding documents, milestone records, dispute resolutions, fork documentation
- Practice catalog with adaptation history

**Archive properties:**
- **Distributed.** Multiple copies across geographies, networks, and storage media. No single point of failure.
- **Version-controlled.** Every change is tracked with rationale and authorship. No silent edits.
- **Publicly accessible.** Any person (movement member or not) can access the full archive.
- **Durable.** Storage formats are chosen for longevity and migration capability. Format migration is a regular maintenance task.
- **Content-addressed.** Documents are verifiable against their canonical versions. Tampering is detectable.

### 6.5 Founder-Independence Mechanisms

The movement is designed to survive, intact, the departure of every person currently involved:

1. **No founder mythology.** The origin of the doctrine is attributed to the ideas, not to the persons. Founders are not named in ceremonial contexts. The doctrine speaks for itself.
2. **Structural, not charismatic, authority.** All authority derives from the documented doctrine, invariants, and governance rules — never from a person's history, reputation, or proximity to the origin.
3. **Deliberate founder dissolution.** If the movement has identifiable founders, they are expected to progressively reduce their institutional role within the first generation, eventually becoming ordinary participants or observers. This is not exile — it is design.
4. **Anti-hagiography principle.** No individual is elevated to special status in movement history. Contributions are acknowledged, but no person is treated as indispensable or uniquely authoritative.
5. **The replaceability test.** Regularly applied: "Does any current decision depend on a specific person's continued involvement?" If yes, this is a structural vulnerability requiring immediate remediation.

### 6.6 Anti-Cult Safeguard Compliance

| Safeguard | Multi-Generational Persistence Implementation |
|-----------|----------------------------------------------|
| Transparency | Full archive is publicly accessible; version-controlled; no silent edits |
| Voluntary exit | Succession planning ensures no one is "trapped" by indispensability |
| No information asymmetry | Archive is complete and public; institutional knowledge is not hoarded |
| Distributed authority | Founder-independence; no hagiography; replaceability test |
| External accountability | Archive is accessible to external observers; history includes mistakes and failures |

## 9. Design Constraints Summary

1. **Doctrine supremacy.** Institutional interests never override doctrinal integrity.
2. **Anti-cult compliance.** All five safeguards are satisfied by every institutional domain.
3. **Anti-othering absolute.** No institutional communication names a human group as adversary.
4. **Subsidiarity.** Decisions default to the most local level capable of making them.
5. **Rotation and term limits.** No permanent governance roles. Every role has defined terms and mandatory gaps.
6. **Recall at every level.** Every governance participant is removable through defined processes.
7. **Firewall.** Advocacy and governance have structural separation to prevent political capture.
8. **Founder-independence.** No institution depends on specific individuals.
9. **Replaceability.** The structural test applies universally: every person must be replaceable.
10. **Public archive.** All institutional records are distributed, version-controlled, and publicly accessible.
11. **No historical figures named.** Structural patterns are absorbed and presented as original design principles.
