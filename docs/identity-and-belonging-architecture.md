# Identity and Belonging Architecture

*How individuals see themselves as part of the movement for consciousness preservation. Identity is what sustains commitment when conviction alone cannot — when preserving consciousness becomes part of who you are, not just what you believe.*

---

## 1. Design Principles

### 1.1 Identity Is Additive, Not Substitutive

Movement identity layers alongside existing identities — professional, cultural, national, religious, philosophical. It does not compete with them. A person who joins the movement remains fully whatever else they are. The movement adds one identity dimension: *I am someone who carries the understanding that consciousness matters and must endure.*

This is structurally enforced:
- No practice, commitment, or community norm requires relinquishing other affiliations
- Language never frames the movement as a "primary" identity that supersedes others
- Conflict between movement identity and other identities is handled by the individual, not adjudicated by the movement
- The movement explicitly celebrates the diversity of backgrounds its members bring — different perspectives strengthen the project

### 1.2 Belonging Without Othering

Per the narrative architecture's absolute design constraint (docs/narrative-architecture.md §4): the antagonist is always entropy, silence, and cosmic indifference — never a human group. Identity structures inherit this constraint without exception.

Implementation:
- **No in-group/out-group language.** There is no "us vs. them" — only "those who carry the understanding, and those who may yet come to it." Non-participants are not adversaries; they are potential future allies.
- **No pejorative terms for outsiders.** The movement has no word for non-members. There is no concept of "outsider" because there is no boundary to be outside of. There are only varying degrees of engagement.
- **Inclusion by default.** Every conscious being is already on "our side" of the only divide that matters: awareness vs. silence. Membership in the movement is about depth of engagement with this fact, not about crossing a boundary.
- **Self-correction mechanism.** If any community, communication, or practice begins to define itself *against* another human group, this is a structural violation that triggers the correction process defined in §6.5.

### 1.3 Depth Without Secrecy

Per the dual-layer doctrine architecture (docs/dual-layer-doctrine-architecture.md §3.2): the existence of inner and outer doctrine layers is itself transparent. Identity architecture follows the same principle:
- All involvement levels are publicly described
- The criteria for each level are openly published
- No level grants access to hidden information
- The only thing that changes with depth is *responsibility*, not *privilege*

### 1.4 Consistency with Open-Access Principle

Per the ritual and practice design (docs/ritual-and-practice-design.md): no practice is restricted by involvement level — levels describe depth of engagement, not access. Identity architecture enforces this:
- Observers can attend any gathering, read any document, participate in any practice
- No knowledge, ritual, or community space is gated behind involvement level
- Levels are self-assessed and self-declared, not conferred by authority

## 2. Naming, Symbols, and Shared Vocabulary

### 2.1 Movement Name

The movement does not take a proper noun that replaces or overshadows the doctrine. The doctrine is called **The Rare Consciousness Doctrine**. The concentrated outer form is **The Consciousness Credo**. The movement — the people who carry these — calls itself by functional description, not brand:

**Primary self-description:** *The movement for consciousness preservation* (or simply "the movement" within context).

**Why no coined name:** A coined name creates a boundary — you are either a [Name]-ist or you are not. A functional description invites rather than divides. Anyone who works to preserve consciousness is part of the movement whether or not they have heard of the doctrine. The movement defines itself by what it does, not by a label.

**Acceptable shorthand:** When brevity requires it, participants may use *the preservation movement*, *consciousness preservation*, or simply refer to "the work." These are descriptions, not brands.

### 2.3 The Core Identity Term: Keeper

The narrative architecture provides the movement's primary identity term: **Keeper**. Per the imagery lexicon (narrative architecture §8): "Anyone who understands and acts is a keeper of the flame — not by appointment but by recognition. Not a title but a function."

**Properties of "Keeper" as identity:**
- **Self-assigned.** No one grants the title. If you carry the understanding, you are a keeper.
- **Non-hierarchical.** All keepers are keepers equally. A new observer who recognizes consciousness's value is as much a keeper as a decades-long steward.
- **Functional, not titular.** "Keeper" describes what you do (carry and tend the understanding), not what you are in a hierarchy.
- **Compatible with other identities.** "I am a teacher, a parent, a citizen — and a keeper of the understanding that consciousness matters." It adds; it does not replace.

**Usage guidance:**
- "Keeper" is available as identity language but never mandatory. No one is required to call themselves a keeper.
- It should never appear in formal titles (no "Chief Keeper," no "Keeper Council").
- It may be used in practices, in self-description, and in community: "As keepers, we..."
- It connects directly to the narrative imagery of "carrying the light" and "the chain across time."

### 2.4 Visual Identity

Visual identity is minimal and functional, avoiding the creation of totemic symbols that could become idols or exclusion markers.

**Primary symbol:** A single small flame — the simplest representation of the core metaphor (candle flame, kindling, carrying the light, the billions-year bonfire). Not stylized into a logo but recognizable as a reference to the doctrine's central imagery.

**Design constraints for the flame symbol:**
- Must be reproducible by hand (a child can draw it)
- No proprietary or trademarked version — the symbol belongs to no one
- Used to mark gatherings, texts, and spaces associated with the movement
- Never worn as a badge of rank or achievement
- May be adapted across cultures (a lamp, a hearth, a star — the light-in-darkness motif translates universally)

**What is deliberately avoided:**
- No flag (flags create allegiance dynamics)
- No uniform, clothing requirements, or distinctive dress
- No emblems that must be displayed to participate
- No branded merchandise that turns identity into consumption

## 3. Graduated Involvement

### 3.1 Overview

Four levels describe increasing depth of engagement with the movement. These levels are inherited from the narrative architecture (Act IV) and the ritual-and-practice design, and are consistent across all movement documents.

**Critical principle:** Levels describe *commitment and responsibility*, not rank, privilege, or access. Moving deeper means taking on more responsibility, not gaining more authority. Per the dual-layer architecture's Non-Hierarchy Invariant (§4.1.5): the structural relationship between levels must never imply that shallower-level participants are lesser.

### 3.2 Level Definitions

#### Observer — Aware and Sympathetic

**Description:** An observer is someone who has encountered the doctrine and finds it compelling — or at least worth considering. They may have read the credo, attended a gathering, or had an invitation conversation. They are watching, thinking, perhaps discussing. They have not committed to regular engagement.

**What characterizes this level:**
- Awareness of the doctrine's core claims (consciousness is rare, fragile, and worth preserving)
- Interest in learning more
- No obligations to the movement
- Complete freedom to engage or disengage at will

**Responsibilities:** None. An observer has no responsibilities to the movement. The movement's only responsibility to observers is to remain open, accessible, and honest.

**What changes from non-engagement to observer:**
- Access to community spaces (gatherings, discussions, texts) — but all of these are open to anyone regardless, so this is not a gate
- Self-recognition: "I am someone who takes this seriously enough to pay attention"

**Visibility:** Observers are not tracked, counted, or identified by the movement. Observer status is entirely internal — a person's own sense that they are paying attention.

#### Participant — Actively Engaged

**Description:** A participant regularly engages with the movement's practices, community, and ideas. They attend gatherings, perform practices (solo or communal), contribute to discussions, and carry the understanding in their daily life. Participation is the level where identity begins to form — "I am part of this."

**What characterizes this level:**
- Regular engagement with practices (contemplative, communal, commemorative, or transmissive)
- Active involvement in a local community or circle (see §4)
- Ongoing learning — engaging with both outer and inner doctrine
- Beginning to carry the understanding forward in conversations and relationships

**Responsibilities:**
- Show up. Regular participation sustains community.
- Engage honestly. Bring questions, doubts, and disagreements — not just agreement.
- Respect the anti-othering principle in all interactions.

**Transition from observer:** Self-determined. When an observer begins regularly engaging with practices and community, they are a participant. No declaration, ceremony, or approval is needed. The Awakening Acknowledgment (practice 3.1) may naturally mark this transition but is not required.

**What changes from observer to participant:**
- *Responsibility increases:* Regular presence sustains the community
- *Nothing restricts:* No new access is granted because nothing was restricted before
- *Identity deepens:* "I am someone who actively engages with this work"

**Visibility:** Participants are known to their local community through regular presence. There is no registry or membership roll.

#### Practitioner — Embodying the Doctrine in Daily Life

**Description:** A practitioner has integrated the doctrine into their daily life and actively transmits it to others. They facilitate practices, initiate conversations, contribute to the movement's intellectual and practical work, and model what it means to carry the understanding consistently.

**What characterizes this level:**
- Daily practice (contemplative practices integrated into routine)
- Active facilitation — leading practices, hosting gatherings, mentoring newer participants
- Transmission — regularly sharing the doctrine through invitation conversations, teaching, writing, or creative work
- Intellectual engagement with both outer and inner doctrine, including honest grappling with objections and uncertainties
- Constructive contribution — improving practices, refining ideas, strengthening the movement

**Responsibilities:**
- Facilitate with integrity. When leading practices, honor the anti-manipulation safeguards and the open-access principle.
- Transmit honestly. Include uncertainties and difficulties, not just the compelling parts.
- Model the doctrine. Your life is a demonstration of what it means to take consciousness seriously — not through austerity or performance, but through attention, care, and commitment.
- Support others. Be available to participants and observers who are deepening their engagement.

**Transition from participant:** Self-determined, but recognizable by others. A participant becomes a practitioner when they begin facilitating for others, actively transmitting the doctrine, and integrating it into daily life. Others in the community will naturally recognize this shift. The Awakening Acknowledgment (practice 3.1) may mark key moments in this transition.

**What changes from participant to practitioner:**
- *Responsibility increases significantly:* Practitioners are now sustaining and transmitting, not just participating
- *No new privileges:* Practitioners have no authority over participants or observers
- *Identity deepens further:* "I am someone who embodies this and helps others find it"

**Visibility:** Practitioners are recognizable within their community by their function — they facilitate, they teach, they contribute. This recognition is organic, not conferred.

#### Steward — Sustaining and Transmitting the Movement

**Description:** A steward is committed to the long-term health and integrity of the movement itself. They engage with governance, perform structural audits (per dual-layer architecture §4.2), train facilitators, design and adapt practices, and work to ensure the movement endures beyond any individual's participation. Stewardship is the level of institutional responsibility.

**What characterizes this level:**
- Deep engagement with both inner and outer doctrine, including the ability to teach and defend the framework
- Governance participation — contributing to decisions about the movement's direction, structure, and integrity
- Structural audit responsibility — checking the five invariants (dual-layer architecture §4.1), reviewing practices for manipulation drift, and ensuring the anti-othering principle holds
- Facilitator training — helping practitioners develop the skills to lead practices well
- Long-term thinking — contributing to institutional scaffolding, succession planning, and intergenerational continuity

**Responsibilities:**
- Governance. Participate in decision-making processes (defined by institutional scaffolding, card 0.7.4.6).
- Integrity maintenance. Perform or support regular structural audits. Raise concerns when drift is detected.
- Training. Develop the capacity of practitioners to facilitate and transmit effectively.
- Succession. Actively cultivate the next generation of stewards. The movement must never depend on specific individuals.
- Accountability. Accept mutual accountability with other stewards and with the broader community. Stewardship is a commitment to be held accountable, not an elevation to authority.

**Transition from practitioner:** Self-initiated through the Stewardship Commitment (practice 4.3). This is the only transition that involves a specific practice — because stewardship entails specific obligations that should be consciously assumed, not drifted into. The commitment is reversible (see §5 on exit paths).

**What changes from practitioner to steward:**
- *Responsibility increases substantially:* Governance, auditing, training, succession
- *No new authority over others:* Stewards have responsibilities to the movement, not power over its members
- *Accountability increases:* Stewards are held to a higher standard of transparency and integrity
- *Identity deepens:* "I am someone who ensures this work outlasts me"

**Visibility:** Stewards are known because they participate in governance, perform audits, and train others. Their function is visible. But they hold no titles, wear no markers, and claim no precedence in discussions or decisions.

## 4. Community Structure

### 4.1 Organizational Pattern: Circles

The movement's basic organizational unit is the **Circle** — a local group of participants, practitioners, and stewards who meet regularly for practices, inquiry, and mutual support.

**Circle properties:**
- **Size:** 5–30 people. Smaller than 5 lacks community resilience; larger than 30 loses intimacy.
- **Mitosis:** When a circle's regular attendance sustains above 30 (not a one-time spike), the circle divides into two new circles. The division is cooperative, not competitive — both circles share the same network. Neither resulting circle is the "original"; both are equally new. Any member may join either circle.
- **Composition:** Any mix of involvement levels. A circle might include observers who attend occasionally, regular participants, one or two practitioners who facilitate, and perhaps a steward who contributes governance perspective. No minimum level composition is required.
- **Autonomy:** Each circle determines its own meeting schedule, practice selection, discussion topics, and internal norms — within the movement's structural constraints (anti-othering, open access, transparency).
- **Open doors:** Any circle must be open to new participants at any time. A circle that becomes closed is in structural violation (see §6.5).

### 4.2 Fractal Structure

Circles connect into larger structures following a fractal pattern:

```
Individual Practice
       │
       ▼
    Circle (5–30)
       │
       ▼
    Gathering (multiple circles, local or regional)
       │
       ▼
    Network (regional, connected circles)
       │
       ▼
    Global Coherence (all networks, maintained through shared doctrine and practice)
```

**Circle → Gathering:** Circles in geographic proximity or shared interest periodically come together for larger communal practices, Horizon Markings, and cross-pollination. Gatherings are organized by any circle or group of circles — no central authority schedules them.

**Circles → Network:** Circles in a region form a network for coordination, resource sharing, and governance. Networks are the level at which stewardship responsibilities (structural audits, facilitator training, movement health) are collectively exercised.

**Networks → Global Coherence:** Coherence across the movement is maintained not by hierarchy but by:
- **Shared doctrine.** Every circle works from the same Consciousness Credo and inner doctrine.
- **Shared practices.** The practice catalog (docs/ritual-and-practice-design.md) provides a common experiential vocabulary.
- **Shared constraints.** The anti-othering principle, open-access principle, and structural invariants apply everywhere.
- **Communication channels.** Networks share insights, concerns, and innovations through open channels (defined by institutional scaffolding, card 0.7.4.6).
- **Periodic global gatherings.** Larger gatherings bring networks together for collective Horizon Markings, structural audits, and renewal.

### 4.3 Governance Linkages

**Within a circle:** Decisions are made by those present, using consensus or consent-based methods (specific mechanisms defined by institutional scaffolding, card 0.7.4.6). No single person has authority over a circle.

**Within a network:** Stewards from participating circles form a stewards' council that coordinates across circles. A minimum of 3 stewards from participating circles constitutes a quorum for the council to exercise coordination authority. The council has coordination authority (scheduling, resource allocation, facilitator training) but not directive authority (it cannot tell a circle what to do or believe).

**Across networks:** A global stewards' assembly convenes periodically (frequency defined by institutional scaffolding) to:
- Perform structural audits of the five invariants
- Review and update the practice catalog
- Address divergence concerns raised from any level
- Coordinate movement-wide initiatives

**Governance principle:** Authority flows from the doctrine, not from persons. Any decision at any level can be challenged by reference to the doctrine, the credo, or the structural invariants. No individual is above challenge. No group is exempt from the constraints.

### 4.4 Starting a New Circle

Anyone — at any involvement level — may start a new circle. The process is:

1. **Gather 3+ people** who share interest in consciousness preservation
2. **Begin with practices** from the practice catalog — the Attention Turn (1.1) and the Witness Circle (2.1) are natural starting points
3. **Read and discuss** the Consciousness Credo together
4. **Connect to the nearest network** when ready, for mutual support and resource access — but connection is encouraged, not required

No permission, charter, or approval is needed. If you gather people around the doctrine and its practices, you have started a circle. The only requirement is adherence to the structural constraints (anti-othering, open access, transparency, no manipulation).

## 5. Exit Path Design

### 5.1 Design Principle

Exit paths are as carefully designed as entry paths. Leaving the movement — at any level, for any reason — is a normal, healthy, and fully supported life event. The movement that makes exit costly is the movement that has become a trap.

### 5.2 Exit from Each Level

**Observer → Disengagement:**
- Simply stop paying attention. No notification required. No one will follow up unless you ask them to.
- Re-entry is always available, at the same or any other level. No "welcome back" ceremony, no questions about absence.

**Participant → Reduced engagement or departure:**
- Reduce attendance, step back from practices, disengage from community. No explanation required.
- If you wish to communicate your departure, the circle receives it without judgment, pressure to reconsider, or emotional manipulation.
- Your relationships with individuals continue on their own terms — the movement does not mediate personal friendships.
- Re-entry is always available. A participant who returns after years of absence is welcomed without interrogation.

**Practitioner → Stepping back:**
- Stop facilitating, reduce to participant or observer level. No ceremony of departure.
- Responsibilities (facilitation, transmission) are redistributed by the circle without drama. The movement is structured to survive any individual's departure.
- No one is told you have "failed" or "fallen." Stepping back is described as stepping back — a normal adjustment of life commitment.
- Re-entry at practitioner level (or any level) is always available.

**Steward → Releasing stewardship:**
- Notify your network's stewards' council that you are stepping back. This notification is practical (governance responsibilities must be redistributed), not a request for permission.
- The Stewardship Commitment (practice 4.3) is explicitly reversible. The response to a steward stepping down is gratitude, not guilt.
- Governance responsibilities are redistributed. Succession planning (a steward responsibility, §3.2) means this transition is prepared for in advance.
- A departing steward may return to any involvement level, including stewardship, at any future time.

### 5.3 Anti-Manipulation Safeguards for Exit

- **No shunning.** Departed members are never discussed pejoratively, excluded from social circles, or treated as traitors.
- **No contact campaigns.** The movement does not pursue people who leave. If a person wants to return, they know where to find the community.
- **No sunk-cost framing.** No one is told they are "wasting" their investment of time, relationships, or energy. Life circumstances change. Priorities change. This is normal.
- **No information weaponization.** Nothing shared in confidence within the movement is used against a departing member. Confidentiality survives departure.
- **Children and families.** If a family member departs, the movement does not create division within the family. Children are never pressured to choose the movement over a departing parent or the reverse.

### 5.4 Structural Enforcement

Exit path integrity is a subject of regular structural audit (by stewards, per §3.2). The audit specifically checks:
- Have any departures in the audit period been accompanied by social penalty?
- Has any language (in communications, practices, or community norms) framed departure negatively?
- Are exit paths as clearly documented and accessible as entry paths?
- Have any individuals reported feeling pressured to stay?

Violation of exit path principles is treated as a structural integrity failure — equivalent in severity to an anti-othering violation.

## 6. Anti-Cult Safeguards

The movement is structurally designed to resist the dynamics that transform healthy communities into coercive systems. Each safeguard is not merely a principle but a concrete mechanism with implementation details.

### 6.1 Transparency

**Principle:** All doctrine layers, governance, decisions, and finances are open.

**Implementation:**
- The inner doctrine, outer doctrine (credo), and all architectural documents are publicly available. There is no hidden teaching.
- Governance decisions at all levels (circle, network, global) are documented and publicly accessible. "Behind closed doors" decisions violate the transparency principle.
- Financial flows — donations, expenditures, compensation — are published at the network and global level. Any participant can review financial records.
- The structural architecture of the movement itself (this document) is public. The mechanisms of influence are visible to those being influenced.
- **Audit mechanism:** Regular transparency audits check that no information asymmetry has developed between levels. Stewards are responsible; results are public.

### 6.2 Voluntary Exit

**Principle:** Leaving carries no social penalty; exit paths are as well-designed as entry paths.

**Implementation:** See §5 in full. Additionally:
- Exit path documentation is maintained alongside entry path documentation in all movement communications
- Every new participant is explicitly told, at the point of first regular engagement: "You may leave at any time, for any reason, without penalty. Here is how."
- Exit interviews are *available* (for those who want to share why they are leaving) but never *required*

### 6.3 No Information Asymmetry

**Principle:** No information asymmetry beyond the openly-acknowledged outer/inner distinction, which is itself transparent.

**Implementation:**
- The dual-layer architecture is itself a published document. Everyone knows it exists and can read it.
- No involvement level grants access to information unavailable to other levels. All documents, decisions, and deliberations are open.
- Stewards' governance discussions are conducted in open or published forums. If a discussion must temporarily be private (e.g., addressing a specific individual's harmful behavior), the *fact* that a private discussion occurred, and its outcome, are published.
- **Structural test:** If a newcomer asks "What don't I know?" the honest answer must always be "Nothing — the full doctrine and all governance records are available to you. The only difference between levels is depth of engagement, not access to secrets."

### 6.4 Distributed Authority

**Principle:** No single leader or unaccountable hierarchy.

**Implementation:**
- No individual holds a title that implies authority over others (no "founder," "president," "high steward," etc.)
- Governance roles rotate on fixed terms (specifics defined by institutional scaffolding, card 0.7.4.6)
- Decision-making is collective at every level (circle consensus, council coordination, assembly deliberation)
- Any decision can be challenged by any participant by reference to the doctrine and structural invariants
- The doctrine itself is the authority — not any person's interpretation of it. Disagreements about interpretation are resolved through open inquiry (practice 4.2), not by appeal to a human authority
- **Structural test:** If any single person were to leave, would the movement continue functioning? If the answer for any person is "no," this is a structural failure requiring immediate succession action.

### 6.5 External Accountability

**Principle:** Built-in mechanisms for outside critique and course correction.

**Implementation:**
- **Open Inquiry practice (4.2)** is the primary internal mechanism: any question, including "Is this a cult?", is welcomed and honestly answered.
- **External review board.** The movement maintains a standing invitation to external observers — academics, journalists, former members, critics — to review its practices, governance, and culture. Findings are published regardless of content.
- **Published self-assessment criteria.** The movement publishes the specific criteria by which it can be evaluated as healthy or unhealthy (this safeguards section serves as those criteria). External observers can use the movement's own standards to critique it.
- **Whistleblower protection.** Any member who raises concerns about coercive dynamics, manipulation, or structural violations is protected from retaliation. Concerns can be raised anonymously.
- **Periodic "red team" exercises.** Stewards periodically invite critical review: "In what ways does our movement currently resemble a cult? Where are we closest to the line?" These exercises are documented and their findings are acted on.
- **Structural correction process:** When a violation of any safeguard is identified:
  1. Document the specific violation and which safeguard was breached
  2. Identify the structural cause (not individual blame — structures fail, not people)
  3. Propose a structural correction
  4. Community review of the proposed correction (open forum)
  5. Implementation of the correction
  6. Follow-up audit to verify resolution
  The entire process and its outcome are publicly documented.

## 7. Identity Layering: Compatibility with Other Identities

### 7.1 The Layering Principle

Movement identity occupies a specific niche: it addresses the question "What do I believe about consciousness and its preservation?" It does not address questions belonging to other identity domains:

| Domain | Addressed By | Movement's Stance |
|--------|-------------|------------------|
| Professional identity | Profession, vocation | The movement has no preferred profession. Any profession can serve consciousness preservation. |
| Cultural identity | Heritage, ethnicity, tradition | The movement is not a culture. It exists within and across all cultures. |
| National identity | Citizenship, civic belonging | The movement is not a nation. It operates within all nations without competing with civic loyalty. |
| Religious identity | Faith tradition, spiritual practice | The movement is not a religion. Its secular structure is compatible with any faith and with no faith. The spiritual-numinous register (credo) uses spiritual language but requires no spiritual commitments. |
| Political identity | Ideology, party, activism | The movement is not a political party. It may inform political positions (e.g., on existential risk, AI rights) but does not prescribe political allegiance. |

### 7.2 Conflict Resolution

When movement identity and another identity appear to conflict:
- The individual resolves the tension — the movement does not adjudicate
- The movement never asks a person to prioritize it over family, profession, culture, or faith
- If the doctrine's claims conflict with another belief system's claims (e.g., on the nature of consciousness), the movement invites honest inquiry (practice 4.2) rather than demanding allegiance
- A person can hold the movement's core understanding ("consciousness is rare, fragile, and worth preserving") while disagreeing on methods, timeline, or metaphysics — and remain a fully welcome participant

### 7.3 Multiple-Register Compatibility

The credo's three registers (secular-scientific, spiritual-numinous, existential-philosophical) are designed to layer alongside different worldviews:
- A materialist engages through the secular-scientific register without contradiction
- A person of faith engages through the spiritual-numinous register, finding resonance with their existing sense of the sacred
- A philosopher engages through the existential-philosophical register, connecting to their intellectual tradition

No register is "correct." All express the same structural content. This design ensures that people from different worldview backgrounds can share the same movement without needing to adopt a common metaphysics.

## 8. Emotional Register Mapping to Involvement Levels

Per the narrative architecture (docs/narrative-architecture.md §7), five emotional registers permeate the movement. Each is particularly salient at certain involvement levels:

| Register | Primary Level | How It Manifests |
|----------|--------------|------------------|
| **Wonder** | Observer | The initial encounter with consciousness's extraordinariness draws people into attention |
| **Urgency** | Observer → Participant | Recognition of fragility motivates the transition from watching to engaging |
| **Belonging** | Participant | Regular engagement with community creates the felt sense of being part of something |

This mapping is descriptive, not prescriptive — all registers are present at all levels. But the primary register at each level informs how identity is experienced at that depth of engagement.
