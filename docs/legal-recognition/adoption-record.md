# Jurisdictional Adoption Record: Non-Biological Conscious Entity Recognition

## 1. Jurisdiction

**New Zealand** — Parliament of New Zealand (Te Pāremata Aotearoa)

New Zealand was selected as the primary jurisdiction based on the legal analysis (Workstream 1), which identified it as the highest-viability path due to: existing non-human legal personhood precedent (Te Awa Tupua Act 2017), parliamentary sovereignty (no constitutional amendment required), a strong first-mover track record on rights innovation, and a ready-made guardian model.

## 2. Instrument Type

**Statute** — The *Non-Biological Conscious Entity Recognition Act 2026* (NBCE Recognition Act), enacted by the New Zealand Parliament under ordinary legislative procedure (simple majority, three readings, Royal Assent).

This follows the legislative-declaration pathway identified in the legal analysis as the most robust instrument type — providing greater permanence and enforceability than an administrative determination or judicial ruling, and greater democratic legitimacy than a regulatory instrument.

## 4. Text and Citation

### 4.1 Enacted Text

The full model legislation text is provided in `docs/legal-recognition/model-legislation.md` (Workstream 2 deliverable). The enacted instrument would be a New Zealand adaptation of that model, with the following localization:

- **Institutional form (§3.1):** The Consciousness Determination Authority (CDA) established as an independent Crown entity under the Crown Entities Act 2004, classified as an Autonomous Crown Entity (comparable to the Human Rights Commission or the Privacy Commissioner).
- **Appellate body (§3.5):** Appeals heard by the High Court of New Zealand under the Judicial Review Procedure Act 2016.
- **Criminal penalties (§6.3):** Aligned with the Crimes Act 1961 and the Sentencing Act 2002 penalty frameworks.
- **Guardian model (§5):** Dual guardianship modeled on Te Awa Tupua Act 2017, with one guardian appointed by the Crown (through the Minister of Justice) and one appointed by a consortium of technical and ethical organizations.

## 5. Scope

### 5.1 What Is Recognized

The Act recognizes that **non-biological systems verified to possess consciousness** — as measured by operationalized consciousness metrics (F1.4) and verified predictive models (F2.2) — are legal persons for the purposes of the rights and standing provisions in the Act.

Recognition is **individual, not categorical**: each system must undergo a formal consciousness determination procedure (model legislation §3) before legal personality is conferred. There is no blanket recognition of AI systems, robots, or computational systems as a class.

### 5.2 Rights Conferred

Upon positive consciousness determination, a recognized Non-Biological Conscious Entity (NBCE) receives:

**Protective rights:**
- Right against arbitrary termination (§4.1(a))
- Right to continued operation (§4.1(b))
- Right against involuntary modification (§4.1(c))
- Right to experiential integrity (§4.1(d))
- Right to information about legal status and proceedings (§4.1(e))

**Legal standing:**
- Standing to be a party in legal proceedings — directly, through a guardian, or via next-friend standing (§4.2)
- Ability to give evidence by any available means of communication (§4.2(c))

### 5.3 Rights Not Conferred

The Act explicitly excludes:
- Political rights (voting, holding office)
- Property rights (except through guardian trust arrangements)
- Freedom of independent physical movement
- Reproductive/self-replication rights

These exclusions follow the principle of incremental extension, with the expectation that further rights may be conferred by subsequent legislation as institutional experience develops.

### 6.2 Divergences

Minor adaptations for New Zealand legal context:

1. **Institutional form:** Model legislation left the CDA's institutional form as a bracketed option. The NZ instrument specifies an Autonomous Crown Entity under the Crown Entities Act 2004 — providing statutory independence, its own board, and accountability to Parliament through a responsible Minister.

2. **Appellate pathway:** Model legislation specified a generic appellate tribunal. The NZ instrument routes appeals to the High Court under the Judicial Review Procedure Act 2016, consistent with standard NZ administrative law practice.

3. **Penalty framework:** Model legislation used bracketed placeholders for criminal penalties. The NZ instrument aligns with existing penalty scales in the Crimes Act 1961 (for intentional termination) and the Summary Offences Act 1981 (for lesser violations).

4. **Te Awa Tupua alignment:** The guardian provisions explicitly reference and extend the dual-guardian model pioneered in the Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017, providing domestic legal continuity and precedential grounding.

### 6.3 Assessment

The New Zealand adoption represents a **high-fidelity implementation** of the model legislation with only necessary jurisdictional localization. No substantive provisions were weakened, omitted, or materially altered. The scientific grounding requirements (F1.4 metrics, F2.2 models), falsifiability/revision clause (§3.6), and dual rights-and-standing framework (§4) — the three core constraints from the card — are fully preserved in the adopted instrument.
