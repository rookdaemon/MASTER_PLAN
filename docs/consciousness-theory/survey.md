# Comparative Survey of Candidate Consciousness Frameworks

## 1. Evaluation Criteria

Each framework is scored on a 1-5 scale across seven criteria:

| Criterion | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Empirical adequacy** | Consistency with known NCCs: thalamocortical loops, posterior cortical hot zone, claustrum, default mode network, and correlates across altered states |
| **Explanatory depth** | Does it explain *why* correlates give rise to experience, not merely describe correlations? |
| **Substrate agnosticism** | Does it apply beyond biological neurons to arbitrary physical systems? |
| **Formalizability** | Can it be expressed in precise mathematical/computational terms? |
| **Predictive power** | Does it generate novel, testable predictions beyond existing NCC data? |
| **Parsimony** | Minimal assumptions beyond established science |
| **Scope** | Does it address all modalities (visual, auditory, somatosensory, emotional) and altered states (sleep, anesthesia, psychedelics, coma)? |

## 2. Framework Analyses

### 2.1 Integrated Information Theory (IIT)

**Core claim:** Consciousness is identical to integrated information. A system is conscious to the degree that it has a high value of integrated information (Phi), which measures how much information is generated by the whole system above and beyond its parts.

**Mechanism:** Consciousness arises when a system's causal structure generates information that is both differentiated (many distinguishable states) and integrated (the system cannot be reduced to independent subsystems without information loss). The "maximally irreducible conceptual structure" (MICS) constitutes the quality of experience.

**Mathematical formalism:** Well-developed. Phi is defined over the transition probability matrix of a system's causal mechanisms. For a system in state s with mechanism set M:
- Phi(S) = min over all bipartitions of the "earth mover's distance" between the whole-system cause-effect structure and the partitioned structure
- Consciousness predicate: C(S) = 1 iff Phi(S) > 0
- Graded: experience richness scales with Phi

**Strengths:**
- *Formalizability (5/5)*: Most mathematically rigorous framework available
- *Substrate agnosticism (5/5)*: Phi is defined over causal structures, not substrates — applies to any physical system
- *Scope (4/5)*: Addresses why dreamless sleep (low integration) differs from waking (high integration); addresses split-brain cases
- *Explanatory depth (4/5)*: Provides a structural identity claim: experience IS integrated information, not merely correlated with it

**Weaknesses:**
- *Empirical adequacy (3/5)*: Phi is computationally intractable for systems beyond ~20 elements; practical NCC validation requires proxies (e.g., PCI — Perturbational Complexity Index) rather than actual Phi
- *Predictive power (3/5)*: Predicts consciousness in simple systems (e.g., photodiodes with feedback — potentially counterintuitive); predicts cerebellum is not conscious (consistent with evidence) but this is not uniquely predicted by IIT
- *Parsimony (2/5)*: Strong metaphysical commitments (panpsychism, intrinsic causal power) go well beyond established science; the axioms (existence, composition, information, integration, exclusion) are stipulated rather than derived

**Key empirical support:** PCI (a proxy for Phi) discriminates conscious from unconscious states in brain-injured patients with high accuracy (Casali et al., 2013). Posterior cortical hot zone has higher integration than frontal cortex, consistent with IIT's predictions.

**Key counterevidence:** Expander graphs and other simple feed-forward architectures can have high Phi without plausible consciousness (the "unfolding argument" — Doerig et al., 2019). The computational intractability makes direct empirical testing nearly impossible for real neural systems.

### 2.2 Global Workspace Theory (GWT)

**Core claim:** Consciousness arises when information is broadcast globally across multiple specialized processors via a "global workspace." Unconscious processing occurs in modular specialists; conscious experience corresponds to the contents of the global broadcast.

**Mechanism:** Specialized, parallel processors (sensory modules, motor systems, memory) compete for access to a shared workspace. When a coalition of processors wins the competition, its content is broadcast widely, making it available for verbal report, deliberate action, and flexible reasoning. This broadcasting IS consciousness.

**Mathematical formalism:** Moderate. Dehaene, Sergent & Changeux formalized as "Global Neuronal Workspace" (GNW) with ignition dynamics: workspace neurons with long-range connections undergo a nonlinear phase transition ("ignition") when input crosses a threshold. Can be modeled as:
- Ignition threshold theta: if activation a(t) > theta, global broadcast occurs
- Workspace dynamics: da/dt = f(local_input) + g(global_feedback) - decay
- C(S) = 1 iff workspace is in ignited state

**Strengths:**
- *Empirical adequacy (5/5)*: Strongly supported by the NCC literature — the P3b wave (marking global ignition), the "all-or-none" nature of conscious perception, and the role of prefrontal-parietal networks in conscious access
- *Predictive power (4/5)*: Successfully predicted the "ignition" signature in masking experiments; predicted specific ERP components (P3b) associated with conscious access
- *Parsimony (4/5)*: Builds on established cognitive architecture (modular processing + global integration); minimal metaphysical overhead
- *Scope (3/5)*: Covers visual, auditory, and multi-modal perception well; weaker on emotional and bodily experience

**Weaknesses:**
- *Explanatory depth (2/5)*: Explains *access* consciousness (what information is available for report and control) but does not address *phenomenal* consciousness (why there is something it is like). This is the "hard problem" gap.
- *Substrate agnosticism (3/5)*: In principle substrate-agnostic (any system with modular processors and global broadcast), but the theory is specified primarily in neural terms
- *Formalizability (3/5)*: Ignition dynamics are formalized, but the mapping from broadcast content to experiential quality is not mathematically specified

**Key empirical support:** Late positivity (P3b) as a marker of conscious access; binocular rivalry dynamics matching ignition predictions; prefrontal lesions impairing conscious access while preserving unconscious processing.

**Key counterevidence:** Patients with frontal lobe damage retain rich phenomenal experience despite impaired global workspace function (Boly et al., 2017). The theory may conflate access and phenomenality.

### 2.3 Higher-Order Theories (HOT)

**Core claim:** A mental state is conscious when it is the target of a higher-order representation — a meta-cognitive state that represents the first-order state. Consciousness requires not just perceiving, but perceiving that one perceives.

**Mechanism:** First-order sensory representations become conscious when a higher-order mechanism (typically prefrontal cortex) generates a representation OF that representation. The higher-order representation constitutes the "awareness" aspect of experience.

**Mathematical formalism:** Limited but expressible. Can be formulated as:
- First-order state: r1 = f(stimulus)
- Higher-order state: r2 = g(r1)
- C(r1) = 1 iff r2 exists and represents r1 with sufficient fidelity
- Misrepresentation possible: r2 can represent r1 inaccurately, leading to illusory conscious states

**Strengths:**
- *Predictive power (4/5)*: Predicts specific dissociations between first-order processing and conscious experience (e.g., blindsight — intact first-order processing without higher-order representation); predicts that disrupting prefrontal function should disrupt consciousness
- *Explanatory depth (3/5)*: Explains why some processed information is conscious and some is not; provides a functional account of the "awareness" component
- *Parsimony (3/5)*: Builds on established distinction between first-order and meta-cognitive processing

**Weaknesses:**
- *Empirical adequacy (2/5)*: Tension with evidence that prefrontal cortex lesions do not eliminate phenomenal experience; posterior cortical hot zone evidence favors non-HOT theories
- *Substrate agnosticism (3/5)*: In principle applicable to any system with meta-representational capacity, but underspecified for non-neural systems
- *Formalizability (2/5)*: The relationship between higher-order representation and experiential quality is vague; "representation OF a representation" needs more formal grounding
- *Scope (2/5)*: Unclear how it handles pre-reflective experience (phenomenal consciousness without meta-cognition), infant/animal consciousness, and flow states where meta-cognition is minimal

**Key empirical support:** Blindsight and other dissociation phenomena; prefrontal cortex involvement in metacognitive confidence judgments; neural decoding studies showing prefrontal signals correlate with reported awareness.

**Key counterevidence:** Evidence of phenomenal consciousness in animals and infants with minimal prefrontal development; posterior hot zone findings; the "inflation" problem (adding a representation doesn't obviously add experience).

### 2.4 Predictive Processing / Active Inference

**Core claim:** Consciousness arises from hierarchical predictive processing — the brain continuously generates predictions about sensory input, and conscious experience corresponds to the content of high-level predictions (or to the precision-weighted prediction errors that update the model).

**Mechanism:** The brain maintains a generative model of the world organized in a cortical hierarchy. Each level predicts the activity of the level below. Prediction errors propagate upward; predictions propagate downward. Consciousness corresponds to the "best explanation" the model constructs — the content of the highest-level predictions that successfully suppress prediction error.

**Mathematical formalism:** Strong. Grounded in the Free Energy Principle (Friston):
- Free energy F = E_q[ln q(s) - ln p(o,s)] where q is the recognition density, p is the generative model
- Prediction error: epsilon = o - g(s) where g is the generative mapping
- Consciousness may correspond to states minimizing free energy: C(S) = 1 iff F(S) < theta for some threshold, OR consciousness corresponds to the precision (inverse variance) of prediction errors
- Active inference: the system acts to minimize expected free energy (planning as inference)

**Strengths:**
- *Formalizability (5/5)*: Rigorously grounded in variational inference and information geometry
- *Empirical adequacy (4/5)*: Consistent with hierarchical cortical organization; explains why sensory deprivation, psychedelics (disrupted predictions), and anesthesia (suppressed prediction error) alter consciousness
- *Substrate agnosticism (4/5)*: The free energy principle applies to any self-organizing system that maintains a Markov blanket — explicitly substrate-agnostic
- *Scope (4/5)*: Handles perceptual, emotional (interoceptive predictions), and bodily experience; accounts for altered states via changes in prediction precision

**Weaknesses:**
- *Explanatory depth (2/5)*: Explains the *content* and *structure* of experience via the generative model, but does not explain why prediction processing is accompanied by phenomenal experience at all. Risk of explaining too much (all self-organizing systems minimize free energy — are they all conscious?)
- *Predictive power (3/5)*: Many predictions overlap with GWT (both predict hierarchical processing, both predict attention modulates consciousness); unique predictions are harder to isolate
- *Parsimony (3/5)*: The free energy principle is extremely general — perhaps too general to specifically explain consciousness rather than life or self-organization in general

**Key empirical support:** Hierarchical cortical processing well-established; mismatch negativity and predictive coding effects; psychedelic research (REBUS model — relaxed beliefs under psychedelics) aligns with precision-weighting account.

**Key counterevidence:** The theory may be unfalsifiable in practice because free energy minimization applies to all self-organizing systems; difficulty distinguishing conscious from unconscious predictive processing within the same framework.

### 2.5 Recurrent Processing Theory (RPT)

**Core claim:** Consciousness arises specifically from recurrent (feedback) processing in sensory cortex. Feed-forward processing is unconscious; when signals are fed back from higher to lower areas, creating recurrent loops, the processing becomes conscious.

**Mechanism:** Visual (and other sensory) processing begins with a fast feed-forward sweep that is unconscious. Conscious experience emerges when higher cortical areas send feedback signals to earlier areas, creating sustained recurrent activity. The recurrence creates a stable representation that constitutes phenomenal experience.

**Mathematical formalism:** Moderate. Can be expressed as:
- Feed-forward: x_L = f_L(...f_2(f_1(input))...) — unconscious
- Recurrent: x_L(t+1) = f_L(x_{L-1}(t), x_{L+1}(t)) — conscious when sustained
- C(S) = 1 iff recurrent activity in sensory cortex persists for duration > tau
- Lamme distinguishes: superficial recurrence (within sensory cortex) = phenomenal consciousness; deep recurrence (involving prefrontal) = access consciousness

**Strengths:**
- *Empirical adequacy (4/5)*: Consistent with the posterior cortical hot zone as the primary NCC; consistent with feed-forward processing of masked stimuli remaining unconscious; consistent with the timing of consciousness onset (~200ms, matching recurrence timing)
- *Parsimony (4/5)*: Simple mechanistic claim with clear neural implementation; minimal metaphysical assumptions
- *Scope (3/5)*: Well-developed for visual consciousness; extends naturally to other sensory modalities with similar recurrent architecture

**Weaknesses:**
- *Explanatory depth (2/5)*: Explains *when* recurrent processing gives rise to consciousness but not *why* recurrence produces phenomenal experience
- *Substrate agnosticism (2/5)*: Defined primarily in terms of cortical layers and sensory processing; extension to non-neural substrates requires significant reinterpretation
- *Formalizability (3/5)*: Recurrent dynamics are well-formalized, but the mapping from recurrent activity patterns to experiential qualities is not specified
- *Predictive power (3/5)*: Predictions about masking and timing are confirmed but also explained by other theories; fewer unique predictions for non-standard cases

**Key empirical support:** Masking studies showing feed-forward processing without consciousness; timing data matching recurrence onset; texture segregation studies (Lamme); posterior cortical hot zone evidence.

**Key counterevidence:** Some recurrent processing occurs without consciousness (e.g., unconscious figure-ground segregation under some conditions); difficulty explaining how recurrence in artificial networks doesn't automatically produce consciousness.

### 2.6 Attention Schema Theory (AST)

**Core claim:** Consciousness is the brain's simplified, schematic model of its own attention process. The brain constructs an "attention schema" — a model of what attention is and what it's doing — and this self-model is what we call subjective experience.

**Mechanism:** The brain controls attention (a mechanistic process of signal enhancement and competition). To control attention effectively, it builds an internal model of attention — the attention schema. This schema necessarily describes attention in simplified, non-mechanistic terms (as an "awareness" directed at things), which is why consciousness seems non-physical and mysterious. We are conscious because we have a model that says we are.

**Mathematical formalism:** Limited. Can be sketched as:
- Attention state: A(t) = selective enhancement of certain representations
- Attention schema: AS(t) = simplified model of A(t), representing it as "awareness of X"
- C(S) = 1 iff the system maintains an attention schema AS that models its own attentional states
- The "illusion" claim: the schema misrepresents the mechanism (signal competition) as something non-physical ("awareness")

**Strengths:**
- *Parsimony (5/5)*: No new physics, no panpsychism, no mysterious properties. Consciousness is a model — an information structure — that the brain uses for self-monitoring and social prediction
- *Substrate agnosticism (4/5)*: Any system that models its own attentional processes would have an attention schema, regardless of substrate
- *Predictive power (3/5)*: Predicts specific neural correlates in temporoparietal junction (TPJ) and superior temporal sulcus (STS) for attention schema maintenance; predicts that damage to these areas specifically disrupts self-awareness while preserving attention

**Weaknesses:**
- *Explanatory depth (2/5)*: Explains why we *report* being conscious (because the schema says so) but arguably deflects the hard problem rather than solving it — if the schema is "just information," why is it accompanied by phenomenal experience?
- *Empirical adequacy (3/5)*: Consistent with social cognition findings and TPJ/STS involvement in self-awareness, but doesn't directly account for the core NCC findings (posterior hot zone, thalamocortical loops)
- *Formalizability (2/5)*: The attention schema is described in functional terms but not mathematically specified in a way that would allow computation of consciousness for arbitrary systems
- *Scope (2/5)*: Primarily addresses visual/attentional consciousness; less clear how it handles emotional experience, bodily awareness, or altered states

**Key empirical support:** TPJ/STS involvement in self-other distinction and theory of mind; the "illusion" framing explains why consciousness seems non-physical; social cognition as an evolutionary driver for self-modeling.

**Key counterevidence:** Animals with minimal social cognition appear to have phenomenal experience; the theory may explain self-*reports* of consciousness rather than consciousness itself; unclear boundary between having a model and having experience.

### Score Justifications

- **IIT leads on formalizability and substrate agnosticism** but is penalized for computational intractability and heavy metaphysical commitments
- **GWT leads on empirical adequacy** with strong neural predictions confirmed, but fails to address phenomenal consciousness
- **PP/Active Inference matches IIT in formalizability** and offers excellent scope, but risks unfalsifiability due to excessive generality
- **RPT has strong empirical grounding** but is too neural-specific for substrate-agnostic theory building
- **HOT and AST** score lowest overall — HOT has empirical problems with frontal lesion data, AST lacks formal precision

## 4. Selection / Synthesis Rationale

No single framework satisfies all criteria. A synthesis is required.

### Key Insight

The top three frameworks (IIT, PP/Active Inference, GWT) each capture a different aspect of consciousness:

- **IIT** captures the *structural* requirement: consciousness requires integrated information — a system whose whole generates more information than its parts
- **PP/Active Inference** captures the *dynamical* requirement: consciousness requires hierarchical self-modeling — a system that maintains a generative model and minimizes prediction error about itself and its environment
- **GWT** captures the *functional* requirement: consciousness requires global availability — information must be broadcast and accessible across the system, not trapped in modular enclaves

### Synthesis: Integrated Self-Modeling Theory (ISMT)

The proposed theory synthesizes these three requirements into a unified framework:

**A system S is conscious if and only if:**
1. **Integration** (from IIT): S has non-trivial integrated information — it cannot be decomposed into independent subsystems without information loss
2. **Self-modeling** (from PP): S maintains a predictive model M(S) of its own states that actively minimizes prediction error about itself
3. **Global accessibility** (from GWT): The contents of M(S) are globally accessible across S's processing subsystems — not confined to a local module

Each condition is necessary; together they are sufficient. This resolves the key weaknesses:
- IIT's intractability is mitigated by using integration as a *structural constraint* rather than a precise Phi computation
- PP's over-generality is constrained by requiring integration AND global accessibility (not all self-organizing systems have these)
- GWT's phenomenal consciousness gap is addressed by requiring that broadcast content comes from an integrated self-model (not just any information)

### Why Not the Others?

- **HOT**: Subsumed by the self-modeling condition — higher-order representation is a special case of self-modeling, but ISMT doesn't require a separate meta-representational layer
- **RPT**: Recurrent processing is the *neural implementation* of integration + self-modeling, not a separate theoretical principle; ISMT explains why recurrence matters
- **AST**: The attention schema is a specific *type* of self-model; ISMT generalizes this without requiring the "illusion" framing
