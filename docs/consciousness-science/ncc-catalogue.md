## 1. Background NCCs — Enabling Conditions for Consciousness

### Reticular Activating System (RAS) / Brainstem Arousal Nuclei
- **Type:** background NCC
- **Modality:** cross-modal
- **Structures:** pontine reticular formation, locus coeruleus (noradrenergic), dorsal raphe nuclei (serotonergic), pedunculopontine/laterodorsal tegmental nuclei (cholinergic), ventral tegmental area (dopaminergic), parabrachial nucleus
- **Key evidence:** lesion studies (Moruzzi & Magoun 1949); brainstem strokes abolish consciousness even with intact cortex; electrical stimulation of RAS produces EEG desynchronization and behavioral arousal
- **Causal status:** necessary
- **Distinction:** prerequisite — arousal enables but does not determine conscious content
- **Open questions:** exact contribution of individual nuclei; role of parabrachial nucleus in valenced consciousness (Fuller et al. 2011)

### Thalamocortical Connectivity
- **Type:** background NCC
- **Modality:** cross-modal
- **Structures:** thalamic relay nuclei (LGN, MGN, VPL/VPM), thalamic reticular nucleus, corticothalamic feedback projections
- **Key evidence:** disruption of thalamocortical loops correlates with loss of consciousness under anesthesia (Alkire et al. 2008); thalamic lesions can produce coma; TMS-EEG complexity measures track thalamocortical integration (Casali et al. 2013)
- **Causal status:** necessary
- **Distinction:** prerequisite — thalamocortical loops are necessary infrastructure for cortical integration
- **Open questions:** whether thalamic activity is merely enabling or constitutive; relative roles of first-order vs. higher-order thalamic relays (Purpura & Schiff 1997)

### Intralaminar Thalamic Nuclei
- **Type:** background NCC
- **Modality:** cross-modal
- **Structures:** centromedian–parafascicular complex, central lateral nucleus
- **Key evidence:** bilateral lesions produce persistent vegetative state; deep brain stimulation of central thalamus can restore behavioral responsiveness in minimally conscious patients (Schiff et al. 2007)
- **Causal status:** necessary
- **Distinction:** prerequisite — supports widespread cortical activation
- **Open questions:** specificity of effect; whether these nuclei gate consciousness or merely arousal

## 2. Content NCCs by Modality

### 2.1 Visual Consciousness

#### Posterior Cortical Hot Zone (Visual)
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** visual
- **Structures:** V1, V2, V3, V4, V5/MT, lateral occipital complex, fusiform face area, parahippocampal place area; temporo-parieto-occipital junction
- **Key evidence:** lesion studies (blindsight in V1 damage — Weiskrantz 1986); binocular rivalry fMRI showing content-specific activation in ventral stream (Tong et al. 1998); no-report paradigms isolate posterior cortex as sufficient for visual experience (Koch et al. 2016)
- **Causal status:** necessary (V1 debated); posterior hot zone as a whole likely sufficient for visual contents
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper for visual contents
- **Open questions:** whether V1 is part of the visual NCC or merely a prerequisite (GNW says prerequisite, IIT says part of NCC); role of feedback from higher areas to V1

#### Recurrent Processing in Visual Cortex
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** visual
- **Structures:** feedback connections from V4/IT back to V1/V2; lateral connections within V1
- **Key evidence:** masking paradigms show that feedforward sweeps alone do not produce conscious perception — recurrence is required (Lamme 2006); TMS disruption of feedback at 100-200ms abolishes awareness but not processing (Pascual-Leone & Walsh 2001)
- **Causal status:** necessary
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper — recurrent processing is proposed as the mechanism distinguishing conscious from unconscious visual processing
- **Open questions:** exact temporal window; whether local recurrence suffices or global recurrence is needed (Lamme vs. Dehaene debate)

#### V5/MT and Motion Awareness
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** visual (motion)
- **Structures:** V5/MT+, medial superior temporal area (MST)
- **Key evidence:** lesions produce akinetopsia (Zihl et al. 1983); TMS to V5 disrupts motion awareness; microstimulation biases motion perception (Salzman et al. 1990)
- **Causal status:** necessary for motion awareness
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper for motion content
- **Open questions:** whether V5 alone is sufficient or requires V1 feedback

### 2.2 Auditory Consciousness

#### Primary and Secondary Auditory Cortex
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** auditory
- **Structures:** Heschl's gyrus (A1), planum temporale, superior temporal gyrus, belt and parabelt regions
- **Key evidence:** lesions to bilateral auditory cortex produce cortical deafness with preserved brainstem auditory evoked potentials; fMRI during auditory masking and attentional paradigms (Gutschalk et al. 2008)
- **Causal status:** necessary for auditory awareness
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper for auditory contents
- **Open questions:** whether auditory awareness requires temporoparietal junction involvement; role of auditory cortex in auditory imagery

#### Temporal Binding in Auditory Cortex
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** auditory
- **Structures:** auditory cortex, superior temporal sulcus; gamma-band synchronization (30-80 Hz)
- **Key evidence:** gamma oscillations correlate with conscious auditory perception in streaming paradigms; MEG studies show temporal binding window of ~30ms for auditory fusion (Joliot et al. 1994)
- **Causal status:** correlational
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper (debated: consequence per theories treating gamma synchrony as epiphenomenal)
- **Open questions:** whether gamma synchrony is causal or epiphenomenal; specificity of frequency bands

### 2.3 Somatosensory Consciousness

#### Primary and Secondary Somatosensory Cortex
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** somatosensory
- **Structures:** S1 (postcentral gyrus — areas 3a, 3b, 1, 2), S2 (parietal operculum)
- **Key evidence:** cortical stimulation produces specific somatosensory experiences (Penfield & Boldrey 1937); lesions produce cortical anesthesia; threshold detection paradigms show S1 activation correlating with awareness (de Lafuente & Romo 2005)
- **Causal status:** necessary for tactile awareness
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper for somatosensory content
- **Open questions:** role of S1 vs. S2 (S2 may be more tightly coupled to awareness)

#### Insular Cortex and Interoceptive Awareness
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** somatosensory (interoceptive)
- **Structures:** posterior insula (primary interoceptive cortex), mid-insula, anterior insula (re-representation for awareness)
- **Key evidence:** Craig's interoceptive pathway model (Craig 2002, 2009); anterior insula activation correlates with subjective feeling states; damage impairs body awareness and emotional experience
- **Causal status:** necessary for interoceptive awareness
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper for bodily/interoceptive consciousness
- **Open questions:** whether anterior insula is NCC-proper or a consequence reflecting metacognitive monitoring; relationship to self-awareness

### 2.4 Emotional / Affective Consciousness

#### Amygdala
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** emotional
- **Structures:** basolateral amygdala, central nucleus, cortical nucleus
- **Key evidence:** amygdala activation in fear conditioning even without conscious awareness (Morris et al. 1998); bilateral amygdala lesions (patient SM) impair fear experience but not all emotion; subcortical visual pathway to amygdala (LeDoux 1996)
- **Causal status:** necessary for fear/threat-related conscious experience (but not sufficient — unconscious processing persists)
- **Distinction:** prerequisite (debated: NCC-proper for fear content per LeDoux 1996; amygdala processes both conscious and unconscious emotional information, suggesting it enables rather than constitutes emotional experience)
- **Open questions:** whether amygdala is NCC-proper or prerequisite for emotional consciousness; subcortical vs. cortical emotion debate (LeDoux vs. Damasio)

#### Anterior Insula and Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC)
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** emotional / affective
- **Structures:** anterior insular cortex (AIC), dorsal ACC (dACC), pregenual ACC
- **Key evidence:** AIC and ACC co-activate across virtually all emotional experiences (meta-analyses: Craig 2009; Critchley et al. 2004); AIC tracks subjective feeling intensity; empathy for pain activates AIC/ACC without somatosensory cortex (Singer et al. 2004)
- **Causal status:** necessary for subjective feeling states
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper for the subjective "feeling" dimension of emotion
- **Open questions:** whether AIC is the seat of all subjective feelings or specific to interoceptive-emotional states; relation to global workspace access

### 2.5 Cognitive / Metacognitive Consciousness

#### Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)
- **Type:** content NCC (disputed — may be consequence)
- **Modality:** cognitive / metacognitive
- **Structures:** dorsolateral PFC (dlPFC), ventrolateral PFC, medial PFC, orbitofrontal cortex
- **Key evidence:** PFC activation in cognitive access, report, and working memory paradigms (Dehaene & Changeux 2011); GNW theory assigns PFC a constitutive role; however, no-report paradigms show PFC activation may reflect report rather than experience (Frässle et al. 2014; Koch et al. 2016)
- **Causal status:** correlational (heavily debated)
- **Distinction:** consequence (debated: NCC-proper per GNW — see Section 6; no-report paradigms, preserved consciousness after lobotomy, and REM dreaming with deactivated PFC favor consequence classification)
- **Open questions:** this is the central open debate; prefrontal lobotomy does not abolish consciousness; prefrontal lesions impair metacognition but not basic phenomenal experience

#### Temporoparietal Junction (TPJ)
- **Type:** content NCC
- **Modality:** cognitive (self/other distinction, agency)
- **Structures:** angular gyrus, posterior superior temporal sulcus, supramarginal gyrus
- **Key evidence:** lesions produce anosognosia, neglect, and out-of-body experiences (Blanke et al. 2002); TPJ activation in theory of mind and self-referential processing; electrical stimulation induces OBEs
- **Causal status:** necessary for self-related aspects of consciousness
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper for self-model/bodily self-consciousness
- **Open questions:** whether TPJ is NCC for self-consciousness specifically or contributes more broadly to spatial consciousness

## 3. Cross-Modal and Integrative NCCs

### Posterior Cortical Hot Zone (Temporo-Parieto-Occipital)
- **Type:** full NCC (candidate)
- **Modality:** cross-modal
- **Structures:** temporo-parieto-occipital cortex; includes posterior regions of temporal, parietal, and occipital lobes; spares prefrontal cortex
- **Key evidence:** identified by Koch, Massimini, Boly & Tononi (2016) as the minimal neural substrate sufficient for conscious experience; within-state no-report paradigms consistently activate this zone; direct cortical stimulation of posterior cortex produces conscious experiences; lesions here (but not in prefrontal cortex) eliminate specific conscious contents
- **Causal status:** sufficient (candidate — strongest current proposal for full NCC)
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper
- **Open questions:** exact boundaries; whether it is truly sufficient without prefrontal involvement; whether it applies to all forms of consciousness including emotional and cognitive

### Claustrum
- **Type:** full NCC (speculative candidate)
- **Modality:** cross-modal
- **Structures:** claustrum — thin sheet of neurons between insular cortex and putamen; extensive reciprocal connections with nearly all cortical areas
- **Key evidence:** Crick & Koch (2005) proposed it as a "conductor of consciousness"; single case report of electrical stimulation producing loss of consciousness (Koubeissi et al. 2014); lesion data limited due to rarity of isolated claustrum damage
- **Causal status:** correlational **[speculative]**
- **Distinction:** uncertain (limited causal evidence; may be prerequisite for cross-modal binding/coordination rather than NCC-proper)
- **Open questions:** very limited causal evidence; may serve as integration hub without being constitutive; recent mouse studies suggest role in attention rather than consciousness (Narikiyo et al. 2020)

### Default Mode Network (DMN)
- **Type:** content NCC (self-referential)
- **Modality:** cross-modal (self, narrative, mind-wandering)
- **Structures:** medial PFC, posterior cingulate cortex/precuneus, angular gyrus, medial temporal lobe
- **Key evidence:** DMN deactivation under anesthesia and in vegetative states (Boly et al. 2008); DMN activity correlates with self-referential thought, mind-wandering, and internal mentation; preserved DMN connectivity distinguishes MCS from VS
- **Causal status:** correlational (necessary for self-related consciousness, not for all consciousness)
- **Distinction:** NCC-proper (debated: prerequisite per theories limiting DMN role to narrative self-consciousness only; NOT required for basic phenomenal consciousness — reduced in meditation and psychedelics without loss of consciousness)
- **Open questions:** whether DMN contributes to consciousness per se or only to self-related content; psychedelic dissolution of DMN with enhanced subjective experience argues against constitutive role

### Global Workspace / Frontoparietal Network
- **Type:** full NCC (candidate per GNW theory)
- **Modality:** cross-modal
- **Structures:** lateral prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, inferior parietal lobule, precuneus; long-range frontoparietal connections
- **Key evidence:** P3b ERP component correlates with conscious access (Sergent et al. 2005); late ignition (~300ms) in frontoparietal network during conscious perception (Dehaene & Changeux 2011); attentional blink and masking paradigms
- **Causal status:** necessary for conscious access (reportable consciousness); disputed for phenomenal consciousness
- **Distinction:** consequence (debated: NCC-proper per GNW — see Section 6; no-report paradigms suggest frontoparietal ignition reflects cognitive access/report rather than phenomenal consciousness)
- **Open questions:** the critical question is whether frontoparietal "ignition" is constitutive of consciousness or merely enables cognitive access/report; no-report paradigms attempt to dissociate these

## 4. NCCs Across Altered States

### 4.1 Sleep Stages

#### NREM Sleep (Stages N1-N3)
- **Consciousness level:** progressively reduced; minimal during N3 (slow-wave sleep)
- **Neural changes:** cortical bistability (UP/DOWN states), reduced thalamocortical connectivity, sleep spindles (N2), high-amplitude slow oscillations (N3)
- **Key evidence:** TMS-EEG shows breakdown of cortical effective connectivity (Massimini et al. 2005); Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) drops below consciousness threshold; dreaming can occur in N2 but is less vivid
- **NCC implications:** loss of consciousness in deep NREM correlates with loss of cortical integration, not cortical deactivation per se; cortex remains active but in a fragmented, bistable regime

#### REM Sleep
- **Consciousness level:** high (vivid dreaming); qualitatively different from waking
- **Neural changes:** cortical activation comparable to waking; PFC hypoactivation (especially dlPFC); pontine cholinergic activation; thalamocortical connectivity restored; PCI values in conscious range
- **Key evidence:** REM dreaming involves rich subjective experience despite PFC deactivation (Hobson et al. 2000); lucid dreaming restores partial PFC activation (Voss et al. 2009)
- **NCC implications:** dreaming with deactivated PFC is strong evidence that PFC is not necessary for phenomenal consciousness; supports posterior cortical hot zone as the primary NCC

### 4.2 General Anesthesia

#### Propofol
- **Consciousness level:** abolished at surgical doses
- **Neural changes:** enhanced GABAergic inhibition; disruption of frontoparietal connectivity; posterior cortical hot zone deactivation at higher doses; thalamocortical disconnection; PCI drops below threshold
- **Key evidence:** Boveroux et al. (2010): progressive loss of frontoparietal then posterior cortical connectivity; graded loss maps to graded loss of consciousness

#### Ketamine
- **Consciousness level:** dissociative — produces unconsciousness at high doses but vivid subjective experiences (k-hole) at moderate doses
- **Neural changes:** NMDA receptor antagonism; preserved or enhanced gamma oscillations; disrupted cortical integration despite maintained cortical activity
- **Key evidence:** ketamine challenges simple "activation = consciousness" models; preserved/enhanced cortical activity with altered consciousness (Mashour & Hudetz 2018)
- **NCC implications:** demonstrates dissociation between cortical activation level and consciousness; integration/complexity rather than raw activation may be key

#### Sevoflurane
- **Consciousness level:** abolished at surgical concentrations
- **Neural changes:** enhanced GABAergic inhibition; disrupted frontal-posterior feedback connectivity; anterior-posterior disconnection
- **Key evidence:** loss of consciousness correlates specifically with loss of feedback connectivity, not feedforward (Ku et al. 2011)
- **NCC implications:** supports recurrent/feedback processing as critical for consciousness

### 4.3 Coma, Vegetative State, Minimally Conscious State

#### Coma
- **Consciousness level:** absent
- **Neural changes:** widespread cortical and subcortical damage or dysfunction; brainstem/thalamic lesions most common cause; absent thalamocortical function
- **Key evidence:** PCI near zero; no response to TMS perturbation; absent EEG reactivity

#### Vegetative State (Unresponsive Wakefulness Syndrome)
- **Consciousness level:** absent (by clinical definition; debated in some patients)
- **Neural changes:** preserved sleep-wake cycles (brainstem function) but severely reduced cortical connectivity; islands of preserved cortical activity possible
- **Key evidence:** some VS patients show fMRI responses to commands (Owen et al. 2006) — suggesting covert consciousness in ~15-20% of clinically diagnosed VS patients; PCI can discriminate

#### Minimally Conscious State (MCS)
- **Consciousness level:** fluctuating, minimal but present
- **Neural changes:** partially preserved cortical connectivity; DMN partially functional; thalamocortical connectivity reduced but not abolished
- **Key evidence:** PCI values in MCS fall in the conscious range (Casarotto et al. 2016); preserved cortical responses to meaningful stimuli; higher functional connectivity than VS
- **NCC implications:** the VS/MCS gradient provides a natural "dose-response" mapping between cortical integration and consciousness level

### 4.4 Psychedelic States

#### Psilocybin / LSD / DMT
- **Consciousness level:** altered, often intensified (not reduced)
- **Neural changes:** 5-HT2A agonism; decreased DMN connectivity and alpha power; increased cortical entropy and repertoire of brain states; decreased modular segregation; increased global functional connectivity (Carhart-Harris et al. 2012, 2016)
- **Key evidence:** Lempel-Ziv complexity of brain signals exceeds normal waking (Schartner et al. 2017); subjective intensity correlates with cortical entropy; dissolution of ego correlates with DMN disruption
- **NCC implications:** consciousness can be intensified while specific networks (DMN, frontoparietal) are disrupted; supports complexity/entropy as a correlate of consciousness level; challenges any theory requiring intact DMN or frontoparietal control for consciousness

### 4.5 Meditation (Advanced Contemplative States)

#### Experienced Meditators
- **Consciousness level:** reportedly heightened clarity; "pure awareness" states
- **Neural changes:** decreased DMN activity during focused attention; increased gamma coherence in long-term practitioners (Lutz et al. 2004); altered anterior insula and ACC activation; possible "minimal phenomenal experience" states with reduced content but preserved awareness
- **Key evidence:** high-amplitude gamma oscillations in experienced monks; self-reports of contentless awareness; neurophenomenological studies (Lutz & Thompson 2003)
- **NCC implications:** meditation states suggest consciousness can exist with minimal content, supporting a distinction between background consciousness (awareness per se) and content consciousness; challenges content-heavy theories

### 4.6 Epileptic Seizures

#### Absence Seizures (Generalized)
- **Consciousness level:** abolished (brief 5-30s)
- **Neural changes:** bilateral 3Hz spike-and-wave discharges; widespread cortical synchronization; thalamocortical hypersynchrony
- **NCC implications:** hypersynchronous cortical activity (low complexity) abolishes consciousness — too much synchrony is as bad as too little; supports complexity/differentiation as key

#### Complex Partial Seizures (Temporal Lobe)
- **Consciousness level:** impaired (focal, with altered awareness)
- **Neural changes:** hypersynchrony in temporal lobe; spread to bilateral networks; inhibition of frontoparietal structures (Blumenfeld et al. 2012)
- **Key evidence:** consciousness lost specifically when seizure activity spreads to bilateral cortex and disrupts subcortical arousal or widespread cortical integration; focal seizures in one hemisphere can preserve some awareness
- **NCC implications:** focal cortical hypersynchrony compatible with altered consciousness; bilateral disruption or arousal disruption required for full loss

## 5. The Prerequisite / NCC-proper / Consequence Distinction

### Framework

Following Chalmers (2000), de Graaf et al. (2012), and Aru et al. (2012), neural processes correlated with consciousness fall into three categories:

1. **Prerequisites**: Neural processes necessary for consciousness to occur but not part of the conscious experience itself (e.g., brainstem arousal, thalamic relay, retinal transduction)
2. **NCC-proper**: The minimal neural substrate whose activity is sufficient for a specific conscious experience; constitutive of the experience
3. **Consequences**: Neural processes that result from conscious experience but are not part of it (e.g., memory encoding, motor preparation for report, metacognitive monitoring)

### Decision Criteria and Experimental Paradigms

| Method | What it isolates | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **No-report paradigms** | Removes consequences tied to report/motor response | Binocular rivalry without response requirements (Frässle et al. 2014) |
| **Within-state manipulations** | Controls for state-level prerequisites | Seen vs. unseen stimuli at matched arousal (contrastive analysis) |
| **Lesion studies** | Tests necessity | Blindsight (V1 lesion → prerequisite); PFC lesion → consciousness preserved |
| **Direct stimulation** | Tests sufficiency | Cortical stimulation eliciting experiences (Penfield; Selimbeyoglu & Parvizi 2010) |
| **Timing analyses** | Distinguishes early prerequisites from NCC from late consequences | ERP components: early sensory = prerequisite; ~200ms recurrence = NCC-proper; P3b (~300ms+) = consequence/access |
| **Matched-content paradigms** | Same content, different awareness | Masking, attentional blink, inattentional blindness |

### Current Consensus Classifications

| Neural Process | Classification | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Brainstem arousal (RAS) | Prerequisite | High |
| Thalamocortical relay | Prerequisite | High |
| Retinal/cochlear transduction | Prerequisite | High |
| V1 feedforward sweep | Prerequisite | Medium (debated) |
| Posterior cortical recurrent processing | NCC-proper | Medium-High |
| Posterior cortical hot zone activation | NCC-proper | Medium-High |
| Anterior insula feeling states | NCC-proper | Medium |
| Frontoparietal ignition (P3b) | Consequence (likely) | Medium (debated — GNW says NCC-proper) |
| Prefrontal report-related activity | Consequence | Medium-High |
| Hippocampal memory encoding | Consequence | High |
| Motor preparation for report | Consequence | High |
| Default mode network (self-referential) | NCC-proper for self-content; prerequisite for narrative self | Medium |

## 6. Open Debates and Discriminating Experiments

### 6.1 Prefrontal vs. Posterior Cortex

**The debate:** Is the prefrontal cortex (PFC) constitutive of consciousness (as GNW claims) or merely involved in cognitive access and report (as IIT and Recurrent Processing Theory claim)?

**Evidence for PFC as NCC-proper (GNW position):**
- Conscious access consistently activates frontoparietal network
- P3b component (frontoparietal) is the most reliable ERP marker of conscious report
- Prefrontal damage impairs some aspects of awareness (extinction, neglect interactions)

**Evidence against PFC as NCC-proper:**
- Prefrontal lobotomy does not abolish consciousness
- REM dreaming involves rich experience with deactivated PFC
- No-report paradigms show PFC activation disappears when report is controlled for
- Children have immature PFC yet rich conscious experience
- Direct stimulation of PFC rarely produces conscious experiences (unlike posterior cortex)

**Discriminating experiments:**
1. **Adversarial collaboration (IIT vs. GNW)**: COGITATE and COGITATE-2 projects — preregistered tests using no-report paradigms, within-state manipulations, and decoded neurofeedback to test whether the NCC for specific contents is frontal or posterior. *Status: results from COGITATE (2023) favor posterior cortex but are not yet definitive*
2. **Full no-report masking**: Test whether backward-masked stimuli that reach PFC but don't produce recurrent posterior processing are consciously experienced. *Prediction: IIT/RPT predict no; GNW predicts yes if PFC ignition occurs*
3. **Brain-computer interfaces**: Record from posterior cortex in patients with complete PFC lesions to determine if reportable (via BCI) conscious experience persists
4. **Intraoperative awareness**: Study cases of awareness under anesthesia with frontal vs. posterior cortical monitoring

### 6.2 Role of Recurrent Processing vs. Feedforward Sweeps

**The debate:** Does the feedforward sweep through visual cortex produce any form of conscious experience, or is recurrent (feedback) processing required?

**Evidence for recurrence requirement:**
- Backward masking disrupts recurrence and abolishes awareness
- TMS applied during feedback window (~100-200ms) disrupts awareness
- Unconscious processing (priming, blindsight) is feedforward-compatible

**Evidence for some feedforward awareness [speculative]:**
- Ultra-rapid categorization (~150ms) may reflect partially conscious feedforward processing
- Some evidence of gist perception before recurrence completes

**Discriminating experiments:**
1. **Precise TMS timing**: Single-pulse TMS during feedforward-only window (50-80ms) vs. recurrence window (100-200ms) to test whether either independently supports awareness
2. **Laminar recordings**: Use laminar electrodes to directly measure feedforward (layer 4 input) vs. feedback (layer 1/5 input) and correlate with trial-by-trial awareness
3. **Optogenetics in animal models**: Selectively silence feedback connections while preserving feedforward flow

### 6.3 Within-State vs. Between-State NCCs

**The debate:** Are the neural correlates that distinguish conscious from unconscious processing *within* a state (e.g., seen vs. unseen masked stimuli during wakefulness) the same as those that distinguish conscious *states* (e.g., wakefulness vs. anesthesia)?

**Current understanding:**
- Within-state NCCs tend to be content-specific (posterior cortex for visual, auditory cortex for sounds)
- Between-state NCCs tend to involve thalamocortical integration, complexity, and arousal
- They may reflect different levels of the same phenomenon (IIT predicts this) or genuinely different mechanisms

**Discriminating experiments:**
1. **Cross-state contrastive analysis**: Apply within-state NCC paradigms (e.g., binocular rivalry) during different background states (wakefulness, light sedation, REM sleep) to test if the same content-NCC operates across states
2. **Graded anesthesia with within-state probes**: Measure posterior cortical NCC markers at different anesthetic depths to determine when within-state NCCs disappear

### 6.4 Proposed Adversarial Collaboration Designs

The field's most productive path forward involves structured adversarial collaborations between competing theories. Key designs:

| Collaboration | Theories Tested | Key Manipulation | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| COGITATE | GNW vs. IIT | No-report paradigms; sustained vs. transient NCCs | Phase 1 complete (2023); ongoing |
| COGITATE-2 | GNW vs. IIT vs. RPT vs. HOT | Expanded paradigm set; animal models | Planned |
| Templeton Foundation program | GNW, IIT, RPT, HOT, Dendritic Integration Theory | Multiple preregistered tests | Active (2019-2025+) |

**Recommended next-generation experiments:**
1. **Decoded neurofeedback**: Train decoders on posterior vs. frontal activity; manipulate each independently to test causal contribution to awareness
2. **Closed-loop brain stimulation**: Real-time TMS/tDCS targeting specific regions during threshold perception tasks
3. **Comparative neurophysiology**: Test NCCs in non-mammalian species (birds, cephalopods) with different brain architectures to identify universal vs. mammal-specific correlates
