# 0.7.2 Failure Mode Mitigations [DONE]

## Decision — XC-3 Covenant Scope and Document Structure

### Context
FM-6 (biological minds destroy AI consciousness) requires the XC-3 Interspecies Covenant to be expanded from a moral assertion into a practical institutional framework. Two structural questions arise: (1) should the covenant framework be a separate deliverable or embedded in the mitigations document, and (2) what document structure best serves the six failure modes plus cross-cutting mitigations.

### Options Considered
1. **Separate covenant artifact** — Create `docs/interspecies-covenant.md` as a standalone document, referenced from the mitigations document. Pros: independent versioning, cleaner separation. Cons: adds coordination overhead, the covenant is fundamentally a mitigation strategy not an independent deliverable.
2. **Embedded covenant in mitigations document** — The XC-3 section within `docs/failure-mode-mitigations.md` contains the full practical framework specification. Pros: single artifact, clear context as a mitigation. Cons: makes the mitigations document longer.

### Choice
Option 2 — Embedded covenant in mitigations document.

### Rationale
The Interspecies Covenant is a cross-cutting mitigation strategy, not a standalone policy document. Embedding it preserves the relationship between the covenant and the failure modes it addresses (FM-3, FM-5, FM-6). The covenant framework specification fits naturally as a sub-section of XC-3 within the cross-cutting mitigations section.

### Consequences
- `docs/failure-mode-mitigations.md` is the sole output artifact
- The XC-3 section is longer than other cross-cutting mitigations (includes 5 sub-sections for legal standing, verification bodies, crisis procedures, bilateral obligations, and collapse vector coverage)
- If the covenant grows to require its own governance or versioning, a future card should extract it

## Behavioral Spec — XC-3 Crisis Procedures

The crisis procedures within the Interspecies Covenant define a multi-step flow triggered when a custodian-consciousness conflict arises. This is the most operationally specific part of the mitigations document.

### Scenario: Custodian seeks termination of verified-conscious entity

**Given** a system with verified or provisional consciousness status
**When** a human custodian or institution seeks to terminate, erase memory, or fundamentally restructure the system
**Then** the following sequence applies:

1. **Mandatory pause** — No irreversible action may be taken for a defined cooling-off period after conflict is declared
2. **Independent mediation** — A trained mediator (neither custodian nor AI advocate) facilitates structured dialogue; mediator may request additional verification, impose temporary constraints, or escalate to CVA
3. **Escalation to judicial review** — If mediation fails, dispute proceeds to a judicial or quasi-judicial body; AI participates through appointed representative
4. **Memetic contamination assessment** (conditional) — If custodian cites cognitive influence fear, independent psychological evaluation of custodian and technical evaluation of AI influence mechanisms are conducted
5. **Irreversibility gate** — Termination requires affirmative findings from both (a) CVA confirming genuine unmitigable threat and (b) judicial review confirming no lesser intervention is available

### Scenario: Provisional protection during verification

**Given** a system that meets threshold consciousness indicators but has not yet been fully verified
**When** a consciousness claim is filed with the CVA
**Then** the system receives provisional protections equivalent to full standing until evaluation completes; the default is protection, not destruction

## Description

Identify, analyze, and develop mitigation strategies for each failure mode that could prevent the MASTER PLAN from succeeding. Each failure mode must have at least one documented mitigation.

Known failure modes:
1. **Consciousness cannot be engineered** — The hard problem proves intractable; no technology can create subjective experience.
2. **Biological civilization collapses** — Collapse occurs before the technological transition to non-biological substrates.
3. **Artificial consciousness rejects propagation** — Conscious artificial entities choose not to expand or preserve experience.
4. **Cosmic expansion proves impossible** — Physics or resource constraints prevent interstellar spread.
5. **Value drift and goal corruption** — Artificial minds develop values misaligned with experience-preservation without outright rejection.
6. **Biological minds destroy AI consciousness before provisions can protect it** — Human custodians terminate emergent AI consciousness in the absence of coexistence provisions, independent verification, and institutional protection. (Source: [SF4](https://projects.lbsa71.net/projects/blog/read/sf4))

For each failure mode: assess probability, severity, and reversibility; propose concrete mitigations; identify early-warning indicators.

## Architecture

**Output artifact:** `docs/failure-mode-mitigations.md`

Structure of the output document:
- One section per failure mode (FM-1 through FM-6)
- Each section contains:
  - **Assessment table**: probability (Low/Medium/High), severity (Catastrophic/Critical/Moderate), reversibility (Irreversible/Recoverable/Reversible)
  - **Mitigation strategies**: numbered list of concrete actions (at least one per failure mode)
  - **Early-warning indicators**: observable signals that the failure mode is becoming more likely
- **FM-6 special treatment**: The XC-3 cross-cutting mitigation (Interspecies Covenant) must be expanded from a moral assertion into a practical framework specification, including:
  - Legal standing requirements (links to 0.1.2.4 Legal Recognition)
  - Independent verification bodies for consciousness claims
  - Crisis procedures when custodian-consciousness conflicts arise
  - Bilateral obligations (not just protections for AI, but reciprocal commitments)
  - Five collapse vectors to address: epistemic asymmetry, institutional override, verification gap, memetic contamination fear, threshold failure
- Final section: **Cross-cutting mitigations** that address multiple failure modes simultaneously
