# 0.4.2.3 Interstellar Propulsion Systems [DONE]

## Description

C2.3 from the Master Plan. Propulsion capable of reaching other star systems within practical timeframes (centuries or less).

Candidate approaches include:

- **Laser sail:** Ground/space-based laser arrays accelerating lightweight sail probes. High initial velocity, minimal onboard fuel.
- **Nuclear pulse propulsion:** Orion-type or fusion-based drives providing sustained thrust. Heavier but self-contained.
- **Antimatter drives:** Highest theoretical energy density. Requires antimatter production and storage infrastructure.
- **Hybrid approaches:** Laser-boosted launch with onboard deceleration drives.

Key design considerations:
- Acceleration and deceleration profiles compatible with probe structural limits
- Fuel/energy requirements vs. payload mass trade-offs
- Deceleration at destination (critical — arrival speed must allow orbital insertion or landing)
- Propulsion components must be fabricable from locally available materials for replication

### Dependencies

- Can be designed in parallel with 0.4.2.2 (resource extraction)
- Informs 0.4.2.1 (probe architectures) mass and energy budgets
- Requires 0.4.1 (planet-independent civilization) for initial launch infrastructure

## Decision

### Primary Propulsion Architecture

**Context:** A propulsion system must deliver 1,000–10,000 kg conscious probes to star systems ≤10 ly away within centuries, decelerate at destination without pre-positioned infrastructure, and be fully replicable from local materials.

**Options Considered:**

1. **Laser sail (acceleration + deceleration):** Requires a pre-positioned laser array at the destination for deceleration — unavailable on first arrival. Ruled out for deceleration.
2. **Nuclear pulse propulsion (full mission):** Self-contained but incurs exponential fuel mass penalty for both acceleration and deceleration at ≥0.05c. Fuel-to-payload ratio ~20:1 round-trip Δv makes payloads impractically small.
3. **Antimatter drives:** Highest energy density but requires antimatter production infrastructure far beyond near-term fabricability. Storage over centuries is an unsolved problem.
4. **Hybrid laser-sail launch + magnetic sail deceleration:** Laser sail eliminates fuel mass for acceleration; magnetic sail uses ambient interstellar medium for deceleration with zero propellant. All materials are common elements.

**Choice:** Option 4 — Hybrid laser-sail acceleration + magnetic sail deceleration, with nuclear pulse backup for low-ISM-density regions.

**Rationale:** Only option that satisfies all constraints simultaneously: no fuel mass penalty for acceleration (laser sail), no destination infrastructure required for deceleration (magsail), all components fabricable from common stellar materials (Si, Al, Y, Ba, Cu, O), and no exotic physics or materials.

**Consequences:**
- Requires construction of a 10–100 GW laser array at each origin system (capital cost, but reusable for many launches)
- Deceleration phase is long (~80–100 years) and dependent on ISM density — mitigated by nuclear backup
- Magsail superconductor must maintain critical current for decades — mitigated by persistent-mode operation at interstellar temperatures (~3 K)

## Contracts

### PropulsionEnvelope (Interface with 0.4.2.1 Probe Architectures)

**Preconditions:**
- Probe payload mass ≤ 10,000 kg
- Probe payload diameter ≤ 50 m
- Probe structural integrity rated for 10g peak acceleration

**Postconditions:**
- Probe delivered to destination star system at ≤ 10 km/s relative velocity
- Transit duration ≤ 200 years for targets ≤ 4.37 ly

**Invariants:**
- Probe experiences no unshielded thermal exposure > 400 K
- Cruise-phase temperature environment: ~3 K (interstellar background)
- Deceleration g-force ≤ 0.001g (magsail regime)

### PropulsionMaterialRequirements (Interface with 0.4.2.2 Stellar Resource Extraction)

**Preconditions:**
- Destination system provides extractable silicon, aluminum, yttrium, barium, copper, oxygen, and hydrogen
- Resource extraction throughput sufficient to supply material quantities within decades

**Postconditions:**
- Complete laser array (emitter modules, reflectors, power generation) fabricated from local materials
- Complete magsail assembly (superconductor wire, deployment mechanism) fabricated from local materials
- If needed: nuclear backup drive and deuterium fuel sourced locally

**Invariants:**
- No materials required that are not among the 30 most abundant elements in stellar/planetary systems
- No isotopic enrichment beyond deuterium extraction from hydrogen

### PropulsionReplicationSpec (Interface with 0.4.2.4 Self-Replication Protocols)

**Preconditions:**
- Replication protocol has access to fabrication blueprints for all propulsion components
- Destination manufacturing capability (per 0.4.2.2) is operational

**Postconditions:**
- Replicated propulsion system passes verification tests: sail reflectivity, magsail critical current, laser beam quality, structural integrity
- Replicated system performance within 5% of original specifications

**Invariants:**
- Replication does not require any components shipped from origin system
- Fabrication complexity classified MEDIUM (no atomic-precision manufacturing required)

## Behavioral Spec

### Mission Phase Sequence

```
Given a probe assembled in an origin star system
When the laser array activates
Then the probe accelerates at 1–10 m/s² for days-to-weeks until reaching cruise velocity (0.05–0.10c)

Given the probe has reached cruise velocity
When the laser array is deactivated
Then the probe enters cruise phase: sail reconfigured as micrometeorite shield, consciousness substrate enters low-power mode, no active propulsion

Given the probe is within deceleration range of destination (~5–15 ly-equivalent distance at cruise speed, i.e. decades before arrival)
When the magsail superconducting loop is deployed
Then the probe decelerates via ISM drag at ≤0.001g over ~80 years

Given the probe's velocity has decreased below orbital insertion threshold (≤10 km/s)
When the probe enters the destination star system
Then the probe performs final orbital maneuvers using destination-star photon pressure or nuclear backup

Given the ISM density along the trajectory is below 0.01 proton/cm³
When the magsail cannot achieve required Δv within 200 years
Then the nuclear pulse backup activates to complete deceleration using onboard deuterium fuel
```
