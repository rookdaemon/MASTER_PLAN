# 0.2.2.1.2 Whole-Brain Scanning [DONE]

## Description

Develop technology capable of scanning a complete biological brain at the resolution determined by 0.2.2.1.1, ideally without destroying the original.

### Key challenges
- Non-destructive scanning at cellular or sub-cellular resolution
- Capturing dynamic state (not just static structure) — synaptic weights, neurotransmitter levels, ongoing activity patterns
- Handling the scale: ~86 billion neurons, ~100 trillion synapses in a human brain
- Data acquisition speed vs. brain state drift during scanning
- Storage and representation of the resulting dataset

## Decision: Primary Scanning Modality

### Context
The upstream fidelity spec (0.2.2.1.1) sets L_min = L2+ (cellular-level, augmented). The scanning requirements are: spatial resolution ≤1 μm, neuron morphology (full 3D arbors), synapse map (pre/post identities, type, weight), receptor distributions, glial topology, and neuromodulatory baseline concentrations. We must select a primary structural scanning modality and a complementary dynamic state capture approach. Non-destructive scanning is preferred but not required if formally justified.

### Options Considered

**O1: Diffusion Tensor MRI + Light-Sheet Microscopy (on cleared tissue)**
- Pros: Light-sheet on cleared tissue (CLARITY/iDISCO) preserves gross structure; MRI is non-destructive for tractography.
- Cons: Resolution ~1–5 μm at best (insufficient for individual synapse resolution); cannot capture receptor distributions; light-sheet cannot resolve dendritic spines reliably at whole-brain scale.
- **Verdict: Insufficient.** Cannot meet ≤1 μm requirement for synapse-level data.

**O2: Serial Block-Face Scanning Electron Microscopy (SBF-SEM)**
- Pros: Proven at ~10–30 nm isotropic resolution on mm³-scale volumes (e.g. MICrONS project, 1 mm³ mouse cortex). Resolves individual synapses, dendritic spines, vesicle pools, and glial processes. Post-processing with immuno-EM compatible for receptor distribution mapping. Well-established segmentation and reconstruction pipelines (Flood-Filling Networks, SEGEM).
- Cons: Destructive — tissue is ablated section-by-section. Slow — current throughput ~10–100 megavoxels/s means a human brain (~1.4×10⁹ mm³ at 30 nm voxels) would require massive parallelization. Generates ~1.5 EB raw data at 30 nm resolution.
- **Verdict: Sufficient with parallelization.** Meets all resolution and feature requirements. Destructiveness must be formally justified.

**O3: Expansion Microscopy + Light-Sheet (ExM-LS)**
- Pros: Physically expands tissue ~4–20× before imaging, turning ~250 nm structures into ~1–5 μm features resolvable by conventional light-sheet. Compatible with fluorescent labeling for receptor typing. Faster acquisition than SBF-SEM.
- Cons: Expansion introduces geometric distortion requiring computational correction. Anisotropic expansion in dense neuropil. Cannot resolve structures below ~50 nm effective resolution even at 20× expansion. Receptor and synaptic weight quantification less established than EM-based methods.
- **Verdict: Complementary but insufficient as primary.** Useful for receptor distribution mapping but cannot match EM for connectivity ground truth.

**O4: Cryo-Electron Tomography (cryo-ET) with FIB-SEM milling**
- Pros: Near-molecular resolution (~2–5 nm). Captures native-state protein conformations. Best preservation of dynamic state at fixation.
- Cons: Extremely slow; current throughput limits to μm³-scale volumes. Not scalable to whole-brain within any foreseeable parallelization. Vastly exceeds the resolution needed for L2+.
- **Verdict: Unnecessarily fine and impractical at scale.**

### Choice
**Primary: SBF-SEM at ~30 nm isotropic resolution, massively parallelized.**
**Complementary: Expansion Microscopy with multiplexed fluorescence (ExM-IF) for receptor distribution mapping on adjacent tissue blocks.**
**Dynamic state: Cryo-vitrification fixation + pre-fixation high-density electrophysiology (Neuropixels-class probes).**

### Rationale
- SBF-SEM is the only proven modality that achieves synapse-level structural resolution at multi-mm³ scale, with established reconstruction pipelines.
- 30 nm resolution exceeds the ≤1 μm requirement by ~33×, providing margin for segmentation accuracy.
- ExM-IF on interleaved blocks provides the receptor distribution data that pure EM cannot capture, satisfying the receptor_distribution requirement.
- Cryo-vitrification captures dynamic state (neurotransmitter concentrations, ion channel conformations) at a defined instant, satisfying the snapshot requirement.
- Pre-fixation electrophysiology provides the activity_recording needed for simulation initialization.

### Consequences
- Scanning is **destructive**: tissue must be sectioned and ablated for SBF-SEM. A formal destruction justification is required (see Behavioral Spec).
- Massive parallelization required: at least ~1000 SBF-SEM instruments operating concurrently to scan a human brain within the drift budget after fixation (tissue stability, not neural drift — fixation freezes state).
- Data pipeline must handle ~1.5 EB raw data with streaming segmentation.
- Two-track tissue processing: alternating blocks go to SBF-SEM vs ExM-IF, requiring precise spatial registration.

## Decision: Output Data Format

### Context
The scan output must be consumable by 0.2.2.1.3 (Neural Simulation) as initialization input. The upstream spec (0.2.2.1.1) requires "structured graph with per-node and per-edge attribute vectors." We must select a concrete serialization format.

### Options Considered

**O1: Custom binary format**
- Pros: Maximum compactness and I/O performance; can be tuned to exact data layout.
- Cons: Requires custom tooling; no ecosystem support; versioning and migration burden.

**O2: HDF5 with NeuroML-inspired schema**
- Pros: Hierarchical, self-describing, supports arbitrary attribute vectors on nodes/edges. Mature tooling (h5py, HDFView). Established precedent in neuroscience (Allen Brain Atlas, Blue Brain Project). Supports chunked I/O and parallel access for PB-scale datasets. NeuroML provides a validated neuroscience data vocabulary.
- Cons: Complex format internals; potential for schema drift without strict versioning.

**O3: Apache Parquet / Arrow**
- Pros: Columnar; excellent for tabular queries. Good for synapse tables.
- Cons: Not naturally hierarchical; awkward fit for graph data with heterogeneous attribute vectors.

### Choice
**HDF5 with NeuroML-inspired schema**, wrapped in a `BrainScanDataset` envelope (as defined in types.ts).

### Rationale
- Best match for the hierarchical, graph-structured data with heterogeneous per-node/edge attributes.
- Proven at neuroscience-scale datasets (Blue Brain Project stores >1 PB in HDF5).
- Chunked parallel I/O supports the streaming acquisition pipeline.
- Schema versioned via `schema_version` field in the dataset envelope.

### Consequences
- The `BrainScanDataset` type in types.ts defines the in-memory representation; HDF5 is the on-disk serialization.
- A schema registry document must define the HDF5 group/dataset layout mapping from the TypeScript types.
- 0.2.2.1.3 must implement an HDF5 reader conforming to this schema.

## Contracts: Scanning Pipeline Interface

### Preconditions
- Fidelity spec from 0.2.2.1.1 is finalized: L_min = L2+, scan_resolution ≤1 μm, required features enumerated.
- Biological brain specimen is available and consented for scanning.
- Scanning infrastructure (SBF-SEM array, ExM-IF pipeline, Neuropixels probes, cryo-vitrification apparatus) is operational.

### Postconditions
The scanning pipeline produces a `BrainScanDataset` (as defined in `src/whole-brain-scanning/types.ts`) that:
1. Contains `structural_data` with resolution_nm ≤ 1000 (≤1 μm), with all sections segmented.
2. Contains `dynamic_state` with activity_recording (pre-fixation), neurotransmitter_map, and synaptic_weight_estimates populated.
3. Contains `timing` where `fixation_completion_time_ms < epsilon_drift_ms`.
4. Contains `destructiveness` with `destruction_justification` populated (scanning is destructive).
5. Passes `validateBrainScanDataset()` with `valid: true`.
6. Passes `ValidationReport` with `overall_pass: true` (cross-modality agreement, detection metrics above thresholds).
7. Is serializable to HDF5 format consumable by 0.2.2.1.3.

### Invariants
- `structural_data.resolution_nm ≤ FIDELITY_RESOLUTION_NM[fidelity_level]` — resolution must always meet or exceed fidelity requirement.
- `timing.fixation_completion_time_ms < timing.epsilon_drift_ms` — fixation must complete within drift budget.
- `validation_checksums` must contain one entry per scanned region, and each checksum must verify data integrity.
- If `is_nondestructive === false`, then `destruction_justification` must be non-null and non-empty.
- `neuron_detection.sensitivity ≥ NEURON_DETECTION_SENSITIVITY_MIN` and `synapse_detection.sensitivity ≥ SYNAPSE_DETECTION_SENSITIVITY_MIN`.

## Threshold Registry

| Name | Value | Unit | Valid Range | Rationale | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| scan_resolution | 1000 | nm | 500–2000 | ≤1 μm per upstream 0.2.2.1.1 spec; must resolve individual synapses and dendritic spines | High — determines modality choice |
| actual_resolution | 30 | nm | 10–100 | SBF-SEM operational resolution; 33× margin over requirement | Low — well within budget |
| epsilon_drift_ms | 100 | ms | 50–500 | Maximum tolerable state drift during fixation; cryo-vitrification achieves <100 ms for small brains, larger brains require perfusion-assisted vitrification | High — constrains fixation protocol |
| pre_fixation_recording_s | 30 | s | 10–300 | Duration of electrophysiology recording before fixation; must capture enough activity for simulation initialization | Medium — longer = better init but more data |
| neuron_detection_sensitivity_min | 0.95 | ratio | 0.90–0.99 | Minimum fraction of true neurons detected by segmentation pipeline | High — missed neurons corrupt connectivity graph |
| neuron_detection_specificity_min | 0.95 | ratio | 0.90–0.99 | Minimum fraction of detected objects that are true neurons | Medium — false positives filterable downstream |
| synapse_detection_sensitivity_min | 0.90 | ratio | 0.85–0.95 | Minimum fraction of true synapses detected | High — missed synapses degrade connectivity |
| synapse_detection_specificity_min | 0.90 | ratio | 0.85–0.95 | Minimum fraction of detected synapses that are true synapses | Medium — false positive synapses add noise |
| weight_estimation_rmse_max | 0.15 | normalized (0–1) | 0.05–0.25 | Maximum RMSE of estimated synaptic weights vs electrophysiology ground truth | High — weight accuracy directly affects simulation fidelity |
| registration_error_max_nm | 500 | nm | 100–1000 | Maximum mean spatial registration error between adjacent sections | High — misregistration severs traced neurites |
| cross_modality_agreement_min | 0.85 | ratio | 0.80–0.95 | Minimum agreement between SBF-SEM and ExM-IF on shared tissue regions | Medium — validates complementary modalities |
| raw_data_size_human_brain | 1.5e18 | bytes | 1e18–2e18 | Estimated raw SBF-SEM data at 30 nm for human brain (1400 cm³) | Low — engineering constraint, not scientific |
| parallel_instruments_min | 1000 | count | 500–5000 | Minimum concurrent SBF-SEM instruments for human-brain-scale throughput | Medium — determines infrastructure scale |

## Behavioral Spec

### Scenario 1: Successful whole-brain scan (inline Given/When/Then)

**Given** a biological brain specimen consented for destructive scanning, a cryo-vitrification apparatus, a pre-fixation electrophysiology rig, and a parallelized SBF-SEM + ExM-IF pipeline

**When** the scanning protocol executes:
1. Pre-fixation electrophysiology recording begins (Neuropixels probes, duration ≥ `pre_fixation_recording_s`)
2. Cryo-vitrification fixation is triggered (completion time < `epsilon_drift_ms`)
3. Fixed brain is sectioned into alternating blocks for SBF-SEM and ExM-IF processing
4. SBF-SEM acquires structural data at `actual_resolution` nm per block (parallelized across ≥ `parallel_instruments_min` instruments)
5. ExM-IF acquires receptor distribution data on interleaved blocks
6. Segmentation pipeline reconstructs neuron morphology, connectivity graph, and synapse map from SBF-SEM data
7. Receptor distributions from ExM-IF are registered and merged with structural graph
8. Dynamic state (neurotransmitter concentrations, synaptic weights) is inferred from structural + receptor data
9. All data is assembled into a `BrainScanDataset` with checksums

**Then**
- `validateBrainScanDataset()` returns `{ valid: true, errors: [] }`
- `ValidationReport.overall_pass === true`
- `neuron_detection.sensitivity ≥ 0.95`
- `synapse_detection.sensitivity ≥ 0.90`
- `weight_estimation_error.mean ≤ 0.15`
- `registration_error_nm.mean ≤ 500`
- Dataset is serializable to HDF5 and loadable by 0.2.2.1.3 simulation pipeline
