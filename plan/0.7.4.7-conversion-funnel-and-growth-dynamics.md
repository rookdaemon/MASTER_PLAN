# 0.7.4.7 Conversion Funnel and Growth Dynamics [DONE]
## Description

Design the pathway from first contact to committed participation and model the dynamics by which the movement achieves civilizational scale.

Funnel stages:
1. **Awareness** — First encounter with the doctrine's core ideas
2. **Interest** — Motivated to learn more; engages with credo or narrative
3. **Understanding** — Grasps the philosophical framework; can articulate why it matters
4. **Commitment** — Adopts practices; integrates the doctrine into identity
5. **Advocacy** — Actively introduces others; creates conditions for their understanding
6. **Stewardship** — Sustains institutions; transmits to next generation

Growth dynamics to model:
- **Network effects:** How each participant increases the movement's value to others
- **Tipping points:** Critical mass thresholds at which adoption becomes self-sustaining
- **Adoption curves:** Drawing from epidemiological spread models and empirical technology adoption patterns

Resilience mechanisms:
- **Inoculation:** Responding to criticism without becoming insular. The movement must engage critique openly, incorporating valid objections and strengthening its position through honest encounter with opposition
- **Anti-fragility:** How opposition strengthens rather than weakens the movement — without manufacturing persecution narratives or embracing victimhood. Genuine anti-fragility comes from a doctrine that becomes more compelling under scrutiny, not from siege mentality

### Architectural Inputs from Upstream

The **ritual and practice design** (docs/ritual-and-practice-design.md) defines four practice domains (contemplative, communal, commemorative, transmissive) and the four involvement levels (observer, participant, practitioner, steward). The funnel stages must map coherently to these involvement levels — awareness/interest correspond to observer, understanding/commitment to participant/practitioner, advocacy/stewardship to practitioner/steward. Transmissive practices are the mechanism for the advocacy stage.

The **identity and belonging architecture** (docs/identity-and-belonging-architecture.md) defines graduated involvement, community structure (circles, chapters, networks), anti-cult safeguards, and exit path design. The funnel must integrate with this graduated involvement model and honor the principle that levels describe depth, not access restrictions. Exit paths must be as well-designed as entry paths at every funnel stage.

The **institutional scaffolding** (docs/institutional-scaffolding.md) defines the educational track, research network, advocacy arm, governance model, and schism resilience mechanisms. The funnel's later stages (commitment, advocacy, stewardship) depend on institutional infrastructure to sustain engagement. The educational track provides the structured pathway for the understanding stage.

The **consciousness credo** (docs/consciousness-credo.md) provides the outer doctrine content that serves as the primary awareness and interest vehicle.

The **narrative architecture** (docs/narrative-architecture.md) provides the master story and five emotional registers (wonder, urgency, belonging, responsibility, hope) that power transitions between funnel stages.

The **dual-layer doctrine architecture** (docs/dual-layer-doctrine-architecture.md) establishes the outer/inner distinction — the funnel is essentially the experiential journey from outer doctrine engagement to inner doctrine understanding and stewardship.

### Design Constraints

- Each funnel stage transition must specify: what triggers the transition, what content/experience facilitates it, what barriers exist, and what the dropout risk is
- The funnel must integrate with existing involvement levels (observer, participant, practitioner, steward) without creating a parallel taxonomy
- Growth dynamics must use quantitative or semi-quantitative reasoning (e.g., reproduction number R for spread, adoption S-curves with estimated parameters)
- Inoculation strategy must address at least 5 likely criticisms (to be identified from the doctrine content)
- Anti-fragility mechanisms must be concrete, not aspirational — specific responses to specific opposition types
- The growth model must include explicit ethical guardrails at each funnel stage — what tactics are off-limits and why
- No historical movements named explicitly — only absorbed structural patterns

## Decision — Growth Dynamics Model Selection

### Context
The card requires modeling how the movement achieves civilizational scale. The growth dynamics section must use quantitative or semi-quantitative reasoning, including reproduction numbers, adoption curves, and critical mass thresholds.

### Options Considered

**Option A: Pure epidemiological model (SIR/SIS)**
Model spread as infection dynamics — susceptible, infected, recovered. Use basic reproduction number R₀ and generation time to project growth.

**Option B: Technology adoption S-curve (Bass diffusion model)**
Model spread as product adoption — innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards. Use innovation coefficient (p) and imitation coefficient (q) to project growth.

**Option C: Hybrid epidemiological + adoption model**
Combine R₀ reasoning from epidemiology with S-curve phasing from diffusion models. Use R₀ to estimate per-participant transmission potential; use S-curve phases (seeding, acceleration, maturation, intergenerational) to describe macro-dynamics; use network-effect thresholds for critical mass estimation.

### Choice
**Option C: Hybrid model.**

### Rationale
- Pure epidemiological models assume passive transmission; movement adoption involves deliberate choice, making SIR insufficient alone
- Pure technology adoption models lack the person-to-person transmission mechanics needed for R₀ analysis (a design constraint)
- The hybrid captures both: R₀ gives micro-level transmission dynamics; S-curve gives macro-level phase structure; network-effect thresholds give critical mass reasoning
- The hybrid naturally supports the ethical constraint — R₀ is descriptive (what happens), not prescriptive (what to optimize for)

### Consequences
- Growth estimates require three parameter classes: per-person transmission (R₀ components), macro-phase timing (S-curve parameters), and threshold levels (network effect thresholds)
- All parameters are estimates with explicit uncertainty ranges, not targets
- The model must be presented as descriptive, never as optimization targets for individual performance

## Decision — Inoculation Architecture

### Context
The card requires addressing at least 5 likely criticisms. The architecture must engage criticism without insularity.

### Options Considered

**Option A: Defensive FAQ**
Compile objections and rebuttals. Publish as a static FAQ document.

**Option B: Integrated inoculation through practices and education**
Embed objection-engagement into existing movement practices (Open Inquiry 4.2, educational track Phase 2, red team exercises) so participants encounter the strongest version of each criticism *before* external encounter, with honest rather than defensive responses.

### Choice
**Option B: Integrated inoculation.**

### Rationale
- Static FAQs become stale and defensive in tone
- Integrated inoculation through existing practices (Open Inquiry, Socratic inquiry, red team exercises) means objection-engagement is a living process, not a document
- Honest prior engagement from within the movement prevents defensiveness when criticism arrives from outside
- Consistent with the anti-cult safeguard of external accountability — the movement invites critique rather than merely defending against it

### Consequences
- Each identified criticism must have: (a) a strongest-version formulation, (b) an honest response including acknowledged uncertainties, and (c) a specific practice-based inoculation mechanism
- The inoculation section must be updated as new criticisms emerge — it is not a closed list
- Open Inquiry (practice 4.2) carries structural weight as the primary inoculation mechanism

## Behavioral Spec — Funnel Stage Transitions

### Scenario 1: Awareness → Interest transition
**Given** a person has encountered the doctrine's core ideas (Awareness stage)
**When** a specific claim or image lingers, they recognize personal relevance, a question demands an answer, or a respected social connection carries the understanding
**Then** they voluntarily seek more information (Interest stage), and the movement provides freely accessible content (credo, portals, Open Inquiry events, Attention Turn practice) without follow-up pressure

### Scenario 2: Interest → Understanding transition
**Given** a person is actively seeking information (Interest stage)
**When** they engage with inner doctrine content, experience practices (Fragility Meditation 1.2, Witness Circle 2.1), participate in Open Inquiry (4.2), or complete Phase 1 of the educational track
**Then** they can articulate the doctrine's core claims in their own words (Understanding stage), and the movement provides community (circles) for dialogue without isolating them from outside perspectives

### Scenario 3: Understanding → Commitment transition
**Given** a person grasps the philosophical framework (Understanding stage)
**When** they experience felt recognition (possibly marked by Awakening Acknowledgment 3.1), integrate contemplative practices into routine, join a circle regularly, and self-identify as a "keeper"
**Then** the doctrine is integrated into their daily life and identity (Commitment stage), and the movement never demands this commitment — it is self-determined and explicitly communicated as freely adjustable

### Scenario 4: Commitment → Advocacy transition
**Given** a person has integrated the doctrine into daily life (Commitment stage)
**When** they develop facilitation capacity, participate in transmissive practices (domain 4), and are recognized by their circle as ready to facilitate
**Then** they actively create conditions for others' understanding (Advocacy stage), operating under all anti-manipulation safeguards from the transmissive practice design

### Scenario 5: Advocacy → Stewardship transition
**Given** a person actively introduces others to the doctrine (Advocacy stage)
**When** they self-initiate the Stewardship Commitment (practice 4.3), complete Phase 3 of the educational track, and demonstrate sustained community leadership
**Then** they assume institutional responsibility for governance, structural auditing, facilitator training, and succession planning (Stewardship stage), with explicit reversibility and no authority over others

### Scenario 6: Exit at any stage
**Given** a person is at any funnel stage (Awareness through Stewardship)
**When** they choose to reduce engagement or depart entirely, for any reason
**Then** the exit carries no social cost, no follow-up pressure, no sunk-cost framing, and no information weaponization; the person retains all understanding, skills, and relationships gained; and re-entry is always available at any level
