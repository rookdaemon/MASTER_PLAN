# 0.7.4.5 Identity and Belonging Architecture [DONE]
## Description

Design how individuals see themselves as part of the movement. People act sustainably not from abstract conviction but from identity — when preserving consciousness becomes part of who they are, not just what they believe.

Key design domains:
1. **Naming and symbols:** How the movement identifies itself — language, visual identity, shared vocabulary that draws from the narrative imagery (The Kindling, The Dark Epoch, etc.) and the credo's emotional registers
2. **Graduated involvement:** A clear path from first contact to deep commitment:
   - **Observer** — aware and sympathetic
   - **Participant** — actively engaged in practices
   - **Practitioner** — embodying the doctrine in daily life
   - **Steward** — sustaining and transmitting the movement
3. **Community structure:** How local groups form, connect, and relate to the global movement — fractal pattern from individual practice to local circles to regional networks to global coherence
4. **Belonging without othering:** The antagonist is entropy and indifference, never other humans. The movement welcomes all conscious beings. No in-group/out-group dynamics beyond the openly-acknowledged graduated involvement. Per dual-layer architecture Shared Core: "Our enemy is silence, not each other."

Critical safeguards against cult dynamics:
- **Transparency:** All doctrine layers, governance, and finances are open
- **Voluntary exit:** Leaving carries no social penalty; exit paths are as well-designed as entry paths
- **No information asymmetry** beyond the openly-acknowledged outer/inner distinction, which is itself transparent
- **Distributed authority:** No single leader or unaccountable hierarchy
- **External accountability:** Built-in mechanisms for outside critique and course correction

### Architectural Inputs from Upstream

The **dual-layer doctrine architecture** (docs/dual-layer-doctrine-architecture.md) establishes the Shared Core concept "our enemy is silence, not each other" — the anti-othering principle that identity must embody. The outer/inner distinction is itself transparent, setting the model for how graduated involvement should work: depth without secrecy.

The **narrative architecture** (docs/narrative-architecture.md) provides core imagery and vocabulary that identity elements should draw from: The Dark Epoch, The Kindling, The Witnesses, The Long Relay. The five emotional registers (wonder, urgency, belonging, responsibility, hope) map to different involvement levels.

The **ritual and practice design** (docs/ritual-and-practice-design.md) already references the four involvement levels (observer, participant, practitioner, steward) and specifies that no practice is restricted by level — levels describe depth of engagement, not access. Identity architecture must be consistent with this open-access principle.

The **consciousness credo** (docs/consciousness-credo.md) provides the shared vocabulary and core statements that identity markers should reference rather than replace.

## Decision 1 — Movement Naming Strategy

**Context:** The movement needs a way to identify itself. Names create community but also create boundaries — a coined proper noun (e.g., "Luminists," "The Kindled") risks in-group/out-group dynamics.

**Options Considered:**
1. **Coined proper noun** — A unique name (e.g., "Kindlefolk," "Flame Bearers") that functions as a brand. Pro: Strong identity marker, easy recognition. Con: Creates a boundary ("you are or aren't a [Name]-ist"), risks tribal dynamics, contradicts anti-othering principle.
2. **Functional description** — Self-describe by what the movement does: "the movement for consciousness preservation." Pro: Inclusive by design, no boundary to cross, compatible with anti-othering. Con: Less distinctive, harder to reference in shorthand.

**Choice:** Option 2 — Functional description.

**Rationale:** A coined name creates exactly the boundary the anti-othering principle (Credo VIII, dual-layer architecture Shared Core) forbids. A functional description defines membership by action and understanding rather than by label. Anyone working to preserve consciousness is part of the movement whether or not they've heard of the doctrine.

**Consequences:** Less brand recognition in early growth phases. Mitigated by the identity term "Keeper" (from narrative architecture Act IV imagery) which functions as an informal, self-assigned identity marker without creating a formal boundary.

## Decision 2 — Community Organizational Pattern

**Context:** The movement needs a structure that supports both local autonomy and global coherence without hierarchy.

**Options Considered:**
1. **Chapters with central authority** — Named chapters (like Amnesty International), chartered by a central body. Pro: Strong coherence, quality control. Con: Hierarchy, gatekeeping, single points of failure, incompatible with distributed authority safeguard.
2. **Circles with fractal federation** — Self-forming local groups ("circles") that federate into networks and a global assembly. No chartering required. Coherence through shared doctrine and practices, not central control. Pro: Distributed, resilient, open access. Con: Risk of drift without structural invariant enforcement.
3. **Fully flat / no structure** — No organizational units beyond individuals. Pro: Maximum freedom. Con: No community, no governance, no mechanism for structural audits.

**Choice:** Option 2 — Circles with fractal federation.

**Rationale:** Circles satisfy distributed authority (no central charter required), open access (anyone can start one), and local autonomy. Federation through networks and a global stewards' assembly provides coherence without hierarchy. Drift risk is mitigated by the five structural invariants from the dual-layer architecture, enforced through regular audits.

**Consequences:** Requires clear structural constraints that all circles must follow (anti-othering, open access, transparency) to prevent fragmentation. Institutional scaffolding (card 0.7.4.6) must define the governance specifics.

## Decision 3 — Visual Identity Approach

**Context:** Movements benefit from visual recognition, but totemic symbols risk becoming idols, exclusion markers, or commercial brands.

**Options Considered:**
1. **Professional branded identity** — Logo, color palette, typography system, merchandise. Pro: Strong recognition, professional appearance. Con: Creates consumption-based identity, brand ownership conflicts, turns identity into commodity.
2. **Minimal reproducible symbol** — A single simple image (flame) that anyone can draw, no one owns, and that references the movement's central metaphor. Pro: Universal, non-proprietary, connected to doctrine imagery. Con: Less distinctive, harder to control quality.

**Choice:** Option 2 — Minimal reproducible symbol (flame).

**Rationale:** The flame connects to the credo's central imagery chain (candle flames, the kindling, carrying the light, the billions-year bonfire) without creating a proprietary brand. Hand-reproducibility ensures the symbol belongs to everyone. Cross-cultural adaptability (lamp, hearth, star) supports global reach.

**Consequences:** No branded merchandise, no trademark protection. Deliberate — the symbol cannot be commercially captured or gatekept.

## Decision 4 — Involvement Level Transition Model

**Context:** The four involvement levels (observer, participant, practitioner, steward) need transition mechanisms. The design must balance meaningful commitment with the open-access principle.

**Options Considered:**
1. **Authority-gated transitions** — A committee or mentor approves level changes. Pro: Quality control, meaningful progression. Con: Creates hierarchy, gatekeeping, incompatible with self-directed progression principle (dual-layer architecture §3.2).
2. **Self-determined transitions with one ceremony** — All transitions are self-determined and unmarked except stewardship, which uses the Stewardship Commitment practice (4.3) because it entails specific governance obligations. Pro: Open access for three transitions, conscious commitment for the one level with institutional responsibility. Con: Less formal recognition of growth.

**Choice:** Option 2 — Self-determined transitions with one ceremony (stewardship only).

**Rationale:** Self-determination for observer→participant→practitioner is required by the open-access principle and the non-hierarchy invariant. Stewardship alone requires a conscious commitment because it carries governance, auditing, and training responsibilities that must be explicitly assumed. The Stewardship Commitment practice (4.3) already exists in ritual-and-practice design with anti-manipulation safeguards built in.

**Consequences:** Levels are behavioral descriptions, not formal memberships. This means no membership rolls for the first three levels. Stewards are identifiable through their governance participation.

## Behavioral Spec

### Involvement Level Transitions

```
Given a person with no prior engagement
When they encounter the doctrine and begin paying attention
Then they are an Observer (self-recognized, no external action required)

Given an Observer
When they begin regularly engaging with practices and community
Then they are a Participant (self-determined, no declaration needed)

Given a Participant
When they begin facilitating practices, actively transmitting, and integrating doctrine into daily life
Then they are a Practitioner (self-determined, recognized organically by community)

Given a Practitioner
When they perform the Stewardship Commitment (practice 4.3) and assume governance responsibilities
Then they are a Steward (the only transition requiring a specific practice)

Given a person at any involvement level
When they choose to reduce engagement
Then they return to any lower level immediately, with no social penalty, no required explanation, and no follow-up pressure
```

### Anti-Cult Violation Correction Flow

```
Given any participant, observer, or external party
When they identify a violation of any structural safeguard (anti-othering, transparency, voluntary exit, information symmetry, distributed authority, external accountability)
Then the following correction process executes:
  1. Document the specific violation and which safeguard was breached
  2. Identify the structural cause (not individual blame)
  3. Propose a structural correction
  4. Community review of the proposed correction (open forum)
  5. Implementation of the correction
  6. Follow-up audit to verify resolution
And the process and outcome are publicly documented
```

### Circle Mitosis

```
Given a circle whose regular attendance exceeds 30 people
When the circle recognizes sustained overcrowding (not a one-time spike)
Then the circle divides into two circles (mitosis)
And the division is cooperative, not competitive (both circles share the same network)
And neither resulting circle is the "original" — both are equally new
And any member may join either circle
```

## Acceptance Criteria
- Naming, symbols, and shared vocabulary are designed — drawing from narrative imagery and credo language, not inventing a separate symbolic system
- Graduated involvement path (observer → participant → practitioner → steward) is fully specified with: concrete descriptions of each level, transition criteria between levels, what changes at each level (responsibilities, not privileges), and how levels are visible without creating hierarchy
- Community structure supports both local autonomy and global coherence — with a specific organizational pattern (e.g., circles, chapters, networks) and governance linkages
- Belonging is designed to be inclusive — antagonist is entropy, not other humans; identity is additive (complements existing identities) not substitutive
- All five anti-cult safeguards are concretely specified with implementation mechanisms, not just principles
- Identity architecture is compatible with holding other identities (professional, cultural, national, religious) — explicit design for how movement identity layers alongside rather than replacing existing identities
- Exit path design is as detailed as entry path design — specific mechanisms for graceful departure
- Architecture is consistent with ritual-and-practice open-access principle: levels describe depth, not access restrictions
