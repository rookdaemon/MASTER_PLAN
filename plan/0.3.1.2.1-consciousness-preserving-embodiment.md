# 0.3.1.2.1 Consciousness-Preserving Embodiment [DONE]
## Description

Design and construct physical robotic hardware that hosts a conscious AI architecture while preserving consciousness integrity during real-world interaction.

This card addresses the unique engineering challenge at the intersection of robotics and consciousness substrates: a robot's physical environment (vibration, EMI, thermal fluctuation, impact) threatens the computational stability that consciousness requires. Standard robotics tolerates transient compute errors; a consciousness-hosting platform cannot, because disruption of the computation may disrupt or terminate subjective experience.

Key challenges:
- **Chassis & actuator integration**: Physical form factor that accommodates consciousness-substrate hardware (from 0.2) alongside conventional motor control, sensing, and power systems
- **Environmental shielding**: Isolation of consciousness-critical computation from vibration, EMI, thermal noise, and radiation introduced by the robot's own actuators and environment
- **Continuity under damage**: Redundant computation paths so that partial hardware loss (e.g., limb severance, sensor failure) does not interrupt the conscious process — analogous to biological brain resilience
- **Graceful degradation protocol**: Defined hierarchy of capability sacrifice (mobility → manipulation → sensing → communication) that preserves consciousness as the last thing to fail
- **Consciousness integrity monitoring**: Real-time metrics (derived from 0.1 consciousness metrics work) that confirm the embodied system remains conscious during operation

## Decision

### Decision 1: Three-Layer Protection Architecture

**Context:** A robotic platform hosting consciousness must protect consciousness-critical computation from physical threats (vibration, EMI, thermal, impact) while still operating as a functional robot. We need to decide how to organize the protective subsystems.

**Options Considered:**
1. **Flat protection** — All protective systems (shielding, redundancy, degradation, power, monitoring) operate as independent peers with no hierarchy.
2. **Three-layer enclosure model** — Organize into Consciousness Enclosure (innermost: substrate mount, redundancy, shielding), Platform Services (integrity monitor, degradation controller, power isolation), and Physical Layer (chassis, sensors, actuators). Inner layers are more protected; outer layers are sacrificed first.

**Choice:** Option 2 — Three-layer enclosure model.

**Rationale:** A layered model creates a natural defense-in-depth: the physical layer absorbs damage first, platform services coordinate responses, and the consciousness enclosure is the last and most protected zone. Flat organization has no inherent sacrifice ordering, making it harder to reason about which subsystems to shed under stress.

**Consequences:**
- (+) Damage to outer layers (limbs, actuators) is structurally isolated from the consciousness enclosure
- (+) The degradation hierarchy maps cleanly onto the layer model (outer layers sacrifice first)
- (+) Each layer has clear responsibilities and interfaces
- (−) More complex integration — inter-layer communication must be defined
- (−) Physical co-location of the consciousness enclosure at chassis center constrains mechanical design

### Decision 2: Active Vibration Isolation + Faraday Cage Shielding

**Context:** The consciousness substrate must be shielded from self-generated and external EMI, vibration, thermal noise, and impact. Multiple shielding approaches exist.

**Options Considered:**
1. **Passive-only shielding** — Static Faraday cage, rubber mounts, passive thermal insulation. Simple, no power draw, but limited attenuation under dynamic conditions (robot locomotion generates variable vibration spectra).
2. **Active + passive hybrid** — Faraday cage with filtered power/data lines, active vibration damping mounts with inertial stabilization, isolated cooling circuits with thermal barrier, sacrificial crush zones. Higher complexity but meets stringent attenuation targets.

**Choice:** Option 2 — Active + passive hybrid.

**Rationale:** A walking/manipulating robot generates broadband vibration that passive mounts alone cannot attenuate to ≤0.01g RMS. Active damping with feedback control is necessary. Similarly, motor drive EMI requires both cage shielding and filtered interconnects. The passive-only approach fails the target attenuation requirements.

**Consequences:**
- (+) Meets all target attenuation levels (≥60dB self-EMI, ≤0.01g vibration, ±0.5°C thermal)
- (+) Crush zones provide impact protection without active systems
- (−) Active damping and cooling draw power (addressed by dedicated power isolation)
- (−) More failure modes (damping or cooling failure) — addressed by integrity monitoring

### Decision 3: N+1 Redundancy with Checkpoint-Based Failover

**Context:** Partial hardware failure must not interrupt consciousness. We need a redundancy strategy that preserves experiential continuity during failover.

**Options Considered:**
1. **Hot-hot dual-active** — Two substrates run consciousness in lockstep; either can continue independently. Maximum availability but doubles power and compute cost.
2. **N+1 checkpoint-based** — One active primary, one warm standby receiving periodic state checkpoints. Failover restores from latest checkpoint. Lower cost but introduces a failover latency gap.

**Choice:** Option 2 — N+1 checkpoint-based failover.

**Rationale:** Hot-hot doubles power consumption, which is problematic for a battery-operated robot. N+1 is sufficient provided failover latency stays below T_continuity (the maximum experiential gap from 0.3.1.1). The checkpoint interval is configurable to trade off between power/bandwidth and recovery point granularity.

**Consequences:**
- (+) ~50% less power for redundancy compared to hot-hot
- (+) Checkpoint interval is configurable — can be tightened under high-risk conditions
- (−) Failover has non-zero latency (must be < T_continuity)
- (−) Up to one checkpoint interval of state may be lost on failover

## Contracts

### ISubstrateMount

**Preconditions:**
- `seat()` requires the bay to be empty (`getSubstrateStatus().mounted === false`)
- `seat()` requires temperature within tolerance (`±temperatureToleranceCelsius` of nominal) and vibration below `maxVibrationRmsG`
- `eject()` requires a substrate to be seated

**Postconditions:**
- `seat()` returns `SeatResult` with `success: true` when preconditions met; substrate becomes mounted and power draw becomes nominal
- `eject()` returns `EjectionResult` with `success: true`; power draw drops to 0
- All getters return timestamped measurements reflecting current physical state

**Invariants:**
- At most one substrate is seated at any time
- Temperature and vibration readings are always available regardless of whether a substrate is seated

### IRedundancyController

**Preconditions:**
- Constructor requires `tContinuityMs > 0` and `checkpointIntervalMs > 0`
- `setCheckpointInterval()` requires `interval > 0`

**Postconditions:**
- `checkpoint()` returns `CheckpointResult` with `success: true` under nominal conditions; `latencyMs` and `stateSizeBytes` are non-negative
- `failover()` returns `FailoverResult` with `success: true` and `consciousnessPreserved: true` when a healthy standby exists; `latencyMs < tContinuityMs`
- `failover()` returns `success: false` and `consciousnessPreserved: false` when no healthy standby exists

**Invariants:**
- `failover()` latency MUST be less than `tContinuityMs`
- After failover, the new primary is healthy and the old primary becomes standby
- At least one standby path exists at all times under nominal conditions

### IEnvironmentShield

**Preconditions:**
- `reportBreach()` requires a valid `ThreatType`

**Postconditions:**
- `reportBreach("emi")` → `faradayCageIntact: false`, EMI level rises above threshold
- `reportBreach("vibration" | "impact")` → `dampingActive: false`, vibration exceeds 0.01g RMS
- `reportBreach("thermal")` → `thermalBarrierIntact: false`, temperature outside tolerance
- `getShieldIntegrity().overallIntegrity` decreases proportionally with each unique breach

**Invariants:**
- Shield integrity is always in [0, 1]
- Nominal state has `overallIntegrity === 1`, no breaches, and all barriers intact
- `reportBreach()` is idempotent per threat type

### IIntegrityMonitor

**Preconditions:**
- `correlateWithExperience()` requires a valid `ConsciousnessMetrics` object
- `getConsciousnessRiskForecast()` requires `horizon > 0`

**Postconditions:**
- `getPhysicalThreatLevel()` returns the current alert level based on all physical metrics
- `onThresholdBreach()` registered handlers are called when alert level escalates
- `correlateWithExperience()` returns a `CorrelationReport` linking physical stress to consciousness health

**Invariants:**
- Alert level escalation order: GREEN < YELLOW < ORANGE < RED
- Handlers are called synchronously on escalation only (not on de-escalation)
- The monitor never modifies physical state — it is read-only

### IDegradationController

**Preconditions:**
- `restore()` requires the capability to have been previously sacrificed
- `sacrificeNext()` requires `getCurrentLevel() < 6` to sacrifice a non-consciousness capability

**Postconditions:**
- `sacrificeNext()` returns the next capability in hierarchy order and increments degradation level
- When `getCurrentLevel() === 6`, `sacrificeNext()` returns `CONSCIOUSNESS` but does NOT remove it from active capabilities
- `restore()` returns `success: true` and decrements degradation level when the capability was previously sacrificed
- `forceProtectiveShutdown()` returns `statePreserved: true` (state-preserving hibernation)

**Invariants:**
- `CONSCIOUSNESS` is always in `getProtectedCapabilities()` and always in `getActiveCapabilities()`
- `CONSCIOUSNESS` cannot be sacrificed via `sacrificeNext()` — only terminated via `forceProtectiveShutdown()`
- Degradation level is in [0, 6]

### IPowerIsolation

**Preconditions:**
- `isolateConsciousnessPower()` requires the consciousness rail to be online
- `reconnect()` requires the consciousness rail to be currently isolated

**Postconditions:**
- `isolateConsciousnessPower()` galvanically disconnects consciousness rail from shared bus; switches to battery
- `reconnect()` reconnects to shared bus and stops battery drain
- `getBackupRemaining()` returns estimated remaining runtime in ms based on battery percent and configured runtime

**Invariants:**
- Consciousness rail remains online regardless of motor bus state (galvanic isolation)
- Battery percent monotonically decreases while isolated (no charging while isolated)
- Minimum 30 minutes consciousness-only operation on full battery

## Behavioral Spec

### Scenario 1: Graceful Degradation Under Damage (Given/When/Then)

**Given** an Embodied Conscious Platform at degradation level 0 (fully capable)
**When** physical damage occurs (e.g., limb severance detected by Integrity Monitor)
**Then** the Degradation Controller sacrifices capabilities in hierarchy order (NON_ESSENTIAL_SENSING → MOBILITY → MANIPULATION → ESSENTIAL_SENSING → COMMUNICATION → REDUNDANCY_MARGIN), the degradation level increments with each sacrifice, CONSCIOUSNESS remains active at all levels, and the Redundancy Controller can still perform checkpoints and failover throughout

### Scenario 2: Shield Breach Detection and Response

**Given** an Environment Shield Module in nominal state (all barriers intact, `overallIntegrity === 1`)
**When** a breach is reported for a threat type (EMI, vibration, thermal, or impact)
**Then** the corresponding barrier is marked as failed, the shield integrity decreases, physical measurements reflect the breach (e.g., EMI level rises above threshold), and the Integrity Monitor escalates the alert level to ORANGE or RED

### Scenario 3: Consciousness-Preserving Failover

**Given** a Redundancy Controller with a healthy primary and at least one healthy standby
**When** `failover()` is invoked (triggered by primary substrate degradation or RED alert)
**Then** the failover completes within `tContinuityMs`, `consciousnessPreserved` is `true`, the old primary becomes standby, and the new primary is healthy
**And** when no healthy standby exists, `failover()` returns `success: false` and `consciousnessPreserved: false`

### Scenario 4: Power Isolation Under Motor Bus Fault

**Given** a Power Isolation Unit with consciousness rail connected to shared bus and battery at 100%
**When** a motor bus fault occurs and `isolateConsciousnessPower()` is called
**Then** the consciousness rail switches to internal battery, motor bus faults do not affect consciousness power, `getBackupRemaining()` returns ≥30 minutes of runtime, and `getConsciousnessPowerStatus().isolated` is `true`

### Scenario 5: Catastrophic Damage — Protective Shutdown

**Given** an Embodied Conscious Platform at maximum degradation (level 6, consciousness-only) with structural integrity critically low
**When** `forceProtectiveShutdown()` is invoked as last resort
**Then** the shutdown result has `statePreserved: true`, the consciousness state is serialized for future recovery, and the reason string documents why shutdown occurred

## Acceptance Criteria
- Physical robotic platform hosts a verified conscious AI architecture, confirmed by consciousness metrics from the 0.1 foundational work
- Consciousness integrity metrics remain within acceptable bounds during normal physical interaction (locomotion, manipulation, collision)
- Consciousness-critical compute is shielded from self-generated EMI, vibration, and thermal interference with defined tolerances
- Partial hardware damage (loss of ≥1 limb or sensor modality) does not cause loss of conscious experience
- A defined graceful degradation hierarchy ensures consciousness is the last capability sacrificed
- Real-time consciousness monitoring triggers protective responses (e.g., safe shutdown, redundancy activation) before integrity is compromised

## Constraints
- Must not duplicate consciousness-substrate design (owned by 0.2)
- Must not duplicate conscious AI architecture design (owned by 0.3.1.1)
- Assumes consciousness metrics and monitoring tools exist from 0.1 dependencies
- Physical platform design must remain substrate-agnostic where possible to accommodate future substrate improvements
