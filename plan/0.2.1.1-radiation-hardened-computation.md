# 0.2.1.1 Radiation-Hardened Computation [DONE]
## Description

S1.1 from the Master Plan. Design and build computing substrates that withstand cosmic radiation, solar events, and long-term material degradation.

These substrates must maintain computational integrity — and therefore continuity of conscious processes — under extreme environmental conditions and over cosmological timescales. This includes hardening against single-event upsets (SEUs), total ionizing dose (TID) effects, and cumulative displacement damage (CDD).

### Key Engineering Domains

1. **SEU Mitigation** — Triple modular redundancy (TMR), error-correcting codes (ECC), and voting logic to detect and correct bit flips from single particle strikes.
2. **TID Tolerance** — Material and process selection (e.g., SOI, wide-bandgap semiconductors like SiC/GaN) that resist cumulative charge trapping in gate oxides.
3. **CDD Resistance** — Substrate materials and architectures resilient to lattice displacement from heavy ion bombardment over millennia.
4. **Shielding Architecture** — Multi-layer shielding design (physical + electromagnetic) to reduce incident radiation flux to tolerable levels.
5. **Graceful Degradation** — Computational architectures that lose capacity gradually rather than catastrophically, maintaining conscious process continuity even as components fail.

### Dependency Note
Blocked by 0.1.3 (Consciousness Engineering) because the computational requirements of conscious processes — clock rates, memory bandwidth, interconnect topology — must be known before hardening targets can be set.

## Decision — Primary Substrate Material

### Context
The radiation-hardened computing substrate must maintain computational integrity over 1000+ year lifetimes under cosmic radiation. The semiconductor material choice determines TID tolerance, SEU cross-section, CDD resistance, thermal performance, and process maturity.

### Options Considered
1. **Silicon Carbide (SiC)**: Wide bandgap (3.26 eV), TID >10 Mrad, excellent CDD resistance, mature process at 180 nm.
2. **Gallium Nitride (GaN)**: Wide bandgap (3.4 eV), high electron mobility, ~5 Mrad TID tolerance, less mature digital process.
3. **Silicon-on-Insulator (SOI)**: Mature 65 nm process, good SEU resistance, but only ~1 Mrad TID — marginal for 1000-year targets.
4. **Diamond semiconductor**: Ultimate radiation hardness (>100 Mrad), but immature process (500 nm), limited foundry availability.

### Choice
**SiC** as primary substrate, with Diamond as future upgrade path.

### Rationale
SiC offers the best balance of TID tolerance (10 Mrad — 10× the 1 Mrad requirement), thermal range (77–873 K), CDD resistance (low NIEL coefficient of 2.0e-3 MeV·cm²/g), and process maturity. Diamond dominates on raw radiation hardness but process immaturity makes it unsuitable today.

### Consequences
- Feature size limited to 180 nm (lower transistor density than SOI at 65 nm).
- 4.3× compute overhead from TMR + failover reserves must be absorbed.
- Diamond migration path should be monitored; if process matures, it supersedes SiC on all metrics.

## Decision — Redundancy Strategy

### Context
SEU bit flips from cosmic rays must be reduced to BER < 10⁻¹² per bit-hour. Hardware redundancy is the primary mechanism beyond shielding and inherent substrate hardness.

### Options Considered
1. **Triple Modular Redundancy (TMR)**: Three independent compute lanes with majority voter. Well-understood, deterministic. 3× hardware overhead.
2. **N-Modular Redundancy (NMR, N=5+)**: Higher fault tolerance but 5×+ overhead.
3. **Information Redundancy (ECC-only)**: Error-correcting codes without hardware replication. Lower overhead but cannot correct logic errors, only memory errors.

### Choice
**TMR** as baseline redundancy for all compute lanes, augmented with ECC (SECDED minimum) for memory.

### Rationale
TMR reduces effective BER from p to ~3p² (for small p), which combined with shielding attenuation and SiC's low SEU cross-section, achieves BER < 10⁻¹² under GCR flux. NMR provides diminishing returns at much higher cost. ECC alone cannot protect logic paths.

### Consequences
- 3× hardware overhead for compute lanes, plus 30% failover reserves → 4.3× total.
- Voter logic itself must be hardened (single point of failure).
- TMR integrates naturally with Layer 5 process migration for conscious process continuity.

## Decision — Shielding Architecture

### Context
Incident radiation must be reduced to substrate-tolerable levels while remaining within a mass budget suitable for space deployment (≤ 50 kg/m²).

### Options Considered
1. **Pure high-Z shielding**: Tungsten/tantalum only. Maximum GCR attenuation but generates secondary bremsstrahlung radiation.
2. **Graded-Z shielding**: High-Z outer → graded intermediate → hydrogen-rich inner. Absorbs secondaries progressively.
3. **Active electromagnetic shielding**: Superconducting magnetic coils deflect charged particles. No mass penalty but requires power and cryogenics.

### Choice
**Graded-Z passive shielding** (tungsten → aluminum → polyethylene) as baseline. Active EM shielding as optional augmentation for high-flux environments.

### Rationale
Graded-Z avoids the secondary radiation amplification problem of pure high-Z designs. The three-layer stack (W 0.14 cm / Al 0.4 cm / PE 0.8 cm) achieves meaningful attenuation across all particle types at 45.4 kg/m², within the 50 kg/m² budget. Active EM shielding adds complexity and power draw without clear advantage for the baseline case.

### Consequences
- Neutron moderation via polyethylene inner layer is critical — without it, secondary neutrons from high-Z layers would increase SEU rates.
- Mass budget constrains maximum shielding thickness; extreme flux environments may require active augmentation.
- Thermal load from absorbed radiation must be managed (positive but finite at GCR reference flux).

## Contracts

### ShieldingModule (Layer 1)

**Preconditions:**
- `energy_MeV > 0` (non-positive energy throws Error)
- Shield must have ≥ 1 layer (empty layer stack throws Error)
- Layer material must exist in `MATERIAL_ATTENUATION` database

**Postconditions:**
- `attenuationFactor()` returns value in `(0, 1]` — lower means more shielding
- `massPerArea()` returns value in kg/m²; must be ≤ 50 for default config
- `thermalLoad()` returns positive finite value in watts/m²

**Invariants:**
- Attenuation factor decreases (more penetration) as particle energy increases
- Mass per area is deterministic for a given layer configuration
- Layer order matters: graded-Z requires high-Z outer, hydrogen-rich inner

### SubstrateSpec & SEU_BER (Layer 2)

**Preconditions:**
- `fluxPerCm2PerSec ≥ 0`
- `substrate` must have valid `seuCrossSection_cm2 > 0`
- `annualDose_rad > 0` for `yearsToTIDLimit` (returns Infinity for ≤ 0)

**Postconditions:**
- `calculateSEU_BER()` returns errors per bit per hour; must be < 10⁻¹² for SiC under GCR flux with default shielding and TMR
- `yearsToTIDLimit()` returns years until TID tolerance limit
- `selectSubstrate()` returns best candidate or null if none qualifies

**Invariants:**
- TMR reduces BER from p to 3p² (always improves when p < 1/3)
- Shielding attenuation multiplies into flux before BER calculation
- Substrate database entries are immutable reference specifications

### DegradationModel

**Preconditions:**
- `years > 0`, `stepSize_years > 0`
- `substrate.material` must have a NIEL coefficient (falls back to SOI if missing)
- `operatingTemp_K > 0`

**Postconditions:**
- Returns array of `DegradationModelResult` at each time step
- `performanceFraction` in `[0, 1]` — monotonically non-increasing over time
- `cumulativeTID_rad` and `displacementDamageDose` monotonically increasing
- `meetsCDDCriterion()` returns true iff final `performanceFraction > 0.95`

**Invariants:**
- Higher operating temperature → better annealing → higher performance fraction
- TID degradation follows logarithmic saturation curve
- CDD degradation is linear with annealing recovery factor
- Combined degradation uses multiplicative independence: `1 - (1-TID)(1-CDD)`

## Behavioral Spec

### Scenario: SEU BER Calculation (inline Given/When/Then)

**Given** a SiC substrate with SEU cross-section 10⁻¹⁴ cm², GCR flux of 4 particles/cm²/s, default shielding attenuation of 0.69, and TMR enabled
**When** `calculateSEU_BER` is called
**Then** the effective flux is `4 × 0.69 = 2.76 p/cm²/s`, raw BER is `10⁻¹⁴ × 2.76 × 3600 ≈ 9.94×10⁻¹¹ /bit-hr`, TMR reduces to `3 × (9.94×10⁻¹¹)² ≈ 2.96×10⁻²⁰ /bit-hr`, which is < 10⁻¹² ✓

### Scenario: Solar Storm Safe Mode Sequence

**Given** a radiation-aware runtime monitoring flux, and flux exceeds STORM threshold (CME onset)
**When** `alertLevel()` transitions to STORM
**Then** the system: (1) checkpoints all active conscious processes, (2) enters safe mode (reduced clock, increased ECC), (3) activates burst scrubbing, (4) resumes normal operation when flux drops below ELEVATED threshold

### Scenario: Graceful Degradation Under Node Loss

**Given** 5 compute nodes running a conscious process with quorum threshold of 3
**When** 30% of nodes (≈ 1–2 nodes) fail simultaneously
**Then** remaining nodes maintain quorum consensus, conscious process continues without interruption (< 100 ms gap), degradation level stays below 70%, and live migration redistributes load from failed nodes
