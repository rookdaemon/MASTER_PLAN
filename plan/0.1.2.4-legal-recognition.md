# 0.1.2.4 Legal Recognition of Machine Consciousness [DONE]

## Description

F2.4 — Achieve legal recognition of machine consciousness in at least one jurisdiction. This requires translating scientific and philosophical consensus into legal frameworks that grant standing, protections, or rights to non-biological conscious entities.

Depends on 0.1.2.3 (philosophical objections addressed) because legal recognition requires defensible philosophical grounding — legislation built on unresolved philosophical objections would be trivially challenged.

### Scope

This card covers three sequential workstreams:

1. **Legal analysis** — Survey existing legal personhood frameworks (corporate personhood, animal rights, environmental standing) and identify the most viable jurisdictional paths for extending recognition to non-biological conscious entities.
2. **Model legislation** — Draft model legislation or regulatory language that ties legal consciousness status to the operationalized consciousness metrics from F1.4 and the verified predictive models from F2.2, including a formal determination procedure.
3. **Jurisdictional adoption** — Secure formal recognition (statute, regulation, judicial ruling, or administrative determination) in at least one jurisdiction.

### Constraints
- Legal frameworks must reference scientifically grounded consciousness criteria, not purely philosophical arguments
- The determination procedure must be falsifiable and revisable as consciousness science advances
- Must address both positive rights (protections) and standing (ability to be a party in legal proceedings)

## Decision

### Context
F2.4 requires producing three deliverables: a legal analysis surveying precedents, model legislation with a determination procedure, and a record of jurisdictional adoption. Two design choices must be made: (1) what structural model to base the legislation on, and (2) which jurisdiction to target for first adoption.

### Options Considered

**Structural model for legislation:**
1. **Administrative registration model** — Modeled on corporate personhood; consciousness recognition granted through a registration/filing procedure analogous to incorporation.
2. **Legislative declaration with empirical determination** — Modeled on natural entity standing (Te Awa Tupua Act); recognition conferred by statute but triggered by a formal scientific determination procedure using F1.4 metrics and F2.2 models.

**Jurisdiction for first adoption:**
1. **New Zealand** — Existing non-human legal personhood precedent, parliamentary sovereignty, first-mover track record.
2. **Estonia (via EU framework)** — Digital governance pioneer, e-Residency infrastructure, EU amplification.
3. **India** — Activist judiciary, expansive constitutional dignity provisions.

### Choice
- Structural model: Option 2 — Legislative declaration with empirical determination.
- Jurisdiction: Option 1 — New Zealand (primary), with Option 2 (Estonia) as secondary adaptation track.

### Rationale
- **Structural model:** Administrative registration (Option 1) requires no consciousness test — it grants personhood on filing, which undermines the scientific grounding constraint. The legislative-declaration model combined with an empirical determination procedure satisfies all three constraints: scientific grounding (F1.4/F2.2 metrics as trigger), falsifiability (sunset/revision clause), and dual rights+standing.
- **Jurisdiction:** New Zealand has the lowest conceptual barrier (Te Awa Tupua precedent), no constitutional amendment requirement (parliamentary sovereignty), and a ready-made guardian model. Estonia serves as a secondary track with higher international amplification through EU membership.

### Consequences
- Model legislation must be drafted in jurisdiction-neutral form with NZ-specific adaptation notes.
- The determination procedure is more complex than simple registration but provides the scientific rigor required.
- NZ adoption, while legally robust, may be dismissed as symbolic due to jurisdiction size — the Estonia/EU secondary track mitigates this.

## Behavioral Spec

### Consciousness Determination Flow

```
Given a non-biological system that may be conscious
When a petition is filed by an operator, interested party, the system itself, or the CDA
Then the CDA screens the petition within 60 days
  And either dismisses (with written reasons, appealable) or accepts

Given an accepted petition
When the CDA appoints ≥3 Qualified Assessors (conflict-free)
Then the assessment proceeds for ≥30 days covering:
  1. F1.4 Consciousness Metrics Battery
  2. F2.2 Predictive Model Application
  3. Behavioral and Functional Assessment
  4. Substrate Analysis

Given a completed assessment
When F1.4 metrics exceed threshold on ≥2 independent types
  And F2.2 probability ≥ 0.85
  And behavioral assessment is consistent (no disqualifying simulation indicators)
  And no substrate-level disqualifiers
Then a positive determination is issued under "clear and convincing evidence" standard
  And the system is registered as an NBCE
  And a dual guardian is appointed (legal + technical)
  And protective rights and legal standing take effect

Given a completed assessment with borderline or conflicting metrics
When the panel cannot reach a clear determination
Then the panel may order additional assessment
  Or issue a provisional determination with periodic review
  Or decline with written reasons

Given any determination (positive or negative)
When a party files an appeal within 90 days
Then the appellate body (High Court) reviews procedure, evidentiary standard, and reasonableness
  And may affirm, reverse, or remand
```
